Who Is an Attorney?

  • An attorney, or lawyer, is a person authorized to engage in the practice of law in the jurisdiction she is in.
  • For the purposes of professional responsibility, the key terms in the preceding definition are "authorized" and "the practice of law."

What is the Practice of Law?

  • What constitutes "the practice of law" is defined by state law. There is no ABA model rule defining what constitutes the practice of law. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.5.
  • States have found practices of law in various situations.

    • Selling Legal Forms

    • A person is permitted to sell legal forms and type up instruments completed by clients, but she cannot provide personal legal assistance, such as correcting errors or omissions by her clients. See Florida Bar v. Brumbaugh, 355 So. 2d 1186 (Fla. 1978).

    Publishing Books about the Law

    • A person is permitted to publish legal text, such as a legal advice book, without engaging in the practice of law. The practice of law requires a person to represent and advise a particular person in a particular situation, as well as a relation of confidence and trust between the persons. See New York County Lawyer's Ass'n. v. Dacey, 234 N.E.2d 459 (N.Y. 1967).

    Providing Legal Software

    • So long as the provided legal software clearly and conspicuously states that they do not substitute an attorney's advice, then the design, creation, publication, distribution, display, or sale of such products does not constitute the practice of law. See Unauthorized Practice of Law Committee v. Parsons Technology, Inc., 179 F.3d 956 (5th Cir. 1999).
  • Where the model rules do provide guidance, however, are the situations where lawyers work with nonlawyers.

Lawyers working with Nonlawyers

  • Generally, if a lawyer has nonlawyer employees, she must supervise the nonlawyer employees in order to avoid unauthorized practice of law. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.3(a); 5.3(b).
Lawyers Supervising Nonlawyers
  • If a lawyer works with a nonlawyer, and the lawyer has direct supervisory authority over the nonlawyer, the lawyer must make reasonably efforts to ensure that the nonlawyer's conduct is compatible with the lawyer's professional obligations. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.3(b).
Nonlawyers Working with Law Firms
  • If a law firm works with a nonlawyer, the law firm's lawyers with managerial authority must make reasonable efforts to ensure that there are measures reasonably assuring that the nonlawyer's conduct is compatible with a lawyer's professional obligations. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.3(a).
Fee Sharing
  • With few exceptions, a lawyer must not share fees with a nonlawyer. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.4(a).

    • Most Common Exceptions

    • A lawyer may include nonlawyer employees in a compensation or retirement plan based on a profit-sharing arrangement. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.4(a)(3).
    • A lawyer may share court-awarded legal fees with a nonprofit organization that employed, retained, or recommended the lawyer's employment in the matter. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.4(a)(4).
Partnering with Nonlawyers
  • Lawyers are prohibited from partnering with nonlawyers if any of the partnership's activities consist of the practice of law. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.4(b).
  • This rule implies that, in any entity engaged in the practice of law, the entity must be headed by a lawyer.
  • This of course does not mean that the entity cannot employ nonlawyers; the entity is free to hire nonlawyers and delegate work to them, so long as a lawyer supervises, and retains responsibility for, the delegated work. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct 5.5, cmt. 2.

Ancillary Business

What Is an Ancillary Business?
  • An ancillary business to a law business is a law-related business or service.
  • Law-related services are services performed in conjunction with, and in substance related to, providing legal services, and are not prohibited as unauthorized practice of law when provided by a nonlawyer. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct R. 5.7(b).
    • Examples of law-related services are provided by Model Rules of Pr. Conduct R. 5.7, cmt. 9:
      • title insurance
      • financial planning
      • accounting
      • trust services
      • real estate counseling
      • legislative lobbying
      • economic analysis
      • social work
      • psychological counseling
      • patent consulting
      • medical consulting
      • environmental consulting

Do Professional Responsibilty Rules Apply to Providing Law-related Services?

  • If a lawyer provides such law-related services, she is subject to professional conduct rules in providing such law-related services if:
    • the law-related services are provided by the lawyer in circumstances not distinct from the lawyer's provision of legal services to clients; or
    • if the lawyer fails to take reasonable measures to assure that the client knowns that the services are not legal services and that the protections of a client-lawyer relationship do not exist, the law-related services provided by an entity controlled by the lawyer, individually or with others.
    • See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.7(a).
General Rule
  • Like fee-sharing, with one exception, a lawyer must not enter into a business transaction with a client or knowingly acquire an ownership, possessory, security, or other pecuniary interest adverse to a client.

Exception

  • A lawyer may enter into a business transaction or knowingly acquire an ownership, possessory, security, or other pecuniary interest with a client provided that all of the following requirements are met:
    • the transaction's or interest acquisition's terms are fair and reasonable to the client;
    • such terms are fully disclosed and transmitted in writing to the client in a manner reasonably understood;
    • the lawyer advises the client, in writing, of the desirability of independent legal counsel on the transaction;
    • the lawyer gives the client a reasonable opportunity seek the advice of independent legal counsel;
    • the client gives informed consent in a writing that:
      • is signed by the client;
      • states the transaction's essential terms;
      • states the lawyer's role in the transaction; and
      • states whether the lawyer is representing the client in the transaction.
    • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct R. 1.8(a)(1)-(3).

Unauthorized Practice of Law

  • To be an attorney, lawyers must be authorized to engage in the practice of law.
  • It follows then that a lawyer is prohibited from engaging in the unauthorized practice of law, for if she is not authorized, she is not a lawyer, and nonlawyers cannot engage in the practice of law.

What Constitutes Unauthorized Practice of Law?

  • If a lawyer is not licensed to practice law in a jurisdiction, she is prohibited from all of the following:
    • establishing an office in the jurisdiction;
    • establishing a systematic presence in the jurisdiction; and
    • holding herself out, or representing, to the public that she is licensed in the jurisdiction
    • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.5(b).
  • Thus, if a lawyer commits any of the above in a jurisdiction she is not licensed in, she has engaged in the unauthorized practice of law.
  • There are, however, exceptions to this rule.

    • Exceptions

    Temporary Practice

    • A lawyer may temporarily provide legal services in an unlicensed jurisidiction, but only if at least one of the following requirements are met:
      • (1) the services are provided in association with a lawyer:
        • licensed to practice in the jurisdiction, and
        • actively participates in the mattter;
      • (2) the services are reasonably related to a pending or potential proceeding before a tribunal in the jurisdiction, and the jurisdiction has authorized the lawyer to appear in such a proceeding
      • (3) the services are reasonably related to a pending or potential arbitration, mediation, or other alternative resolution proceeding, provided that:
        • the services are reasonably related to the lawyer's practice in her licensed jurisdiction, and
        • the unlicensed jurisdiction does not require the lawyer's pro hac vice admission to provide such services; or
      • (4) only if exceptions (2) and (3) do not apply, the services arise out of or are reasonably related to the lawyer's licensed jurisdiction.

    In-House Counsel Exception

    • As long as a lawyer is not disbarred or suspended from the jurisdiction she is admitted in, she can engage in the practice of law in an unlicensed jurisdiction as an in-house counsel for company in that jurisdiction. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.5(d)(1).

    Federal Services Practice Exception

    • Similarly, as long as a lawyer is not disbarred or suspended from the jurisdiction she is admitted in, she can provide federal legal services in an unlicensed jurisdiction (assuming that she is authorized by federal law or rules to provide such federal legal services). See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 5.5(d)(2).

    Assuming that none of these issues are present, the focus of the professional responsibility rules then turns to the attorney-client relationship.

    Attorney-Client Relationship: Conception

    Lawyer's Duty to Reject Certain Cases

    • In certain situations, a lawyer facing a prospective client has a duty to reject representation.
    • While a lawyer meets a prospective client, if the lawyer knows, or reasonably should know, that (a) representing the client will violate the the Rules of Professional Conduct or other law, or (b) the lawyer's physical or mental condition materially impairs the lawyer's ability to represent the client, then the lawyer must reject representation. Model Rule 1.16(a).
    • A lawyer must not bring or defend a proceeding, or assert or controvert an issue in that proceeding, unless doing so has a basis in law for fact. Model Rule 3.1.
    • A lawyer must not counsel a client to engage, or assist a client, in conduct that the lawyer knows is criminal or fraudulent. Model Rule 1.2(d).

    Attorney-Client Relationship: Life

    We've established an attorney client relationship. What rules apply during the attorney client relationship?

    The Lawyer's Duty of Competence

    • A lawyer must provide competent representation to a client; such provision requires the legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation reasonably necessary for the representation. Model Rule 1.1.

    The lawyer's duty of competence extends to the ways in which she practices. A lawyer can easily inadvertently violate her duty of competence in her conducting routine tasks.

    Whether a Lawyer Has the Requisite Legal Knowledge or Skill

    • Because providing competent representation requires reasonably necessary legal knowledge, a lawyer may be limited to handling matters that involve subjects she studied during law school. Model Rule 1.1, cmt. 8.
    • In determining whether a lawyer employs the requisite knowledge or skill in a particular matter, relevant factors include:
      • the relative complexity and specialized nature of the matter;
      • the lawyer's general experience;
      • the lawyer's training and experience in the field in question;
      • the preparation and study the lawyer is able to give the matter, and whether it is feasible to refer the matter to, or consult with, a lawyer of established competence in the field.
      • Model Rule 1.1, cmt. 1.

    Duty of Competence & Technology

    • A lawyer must keep abrest of the benefits and risks associated with relevant technology. Model Rule 1.1, cmt. 8.

    Conflicts of Interest

    The Right to Conflict-Free Representation

    Under the 6th Amendment:

    In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right to a speed and public trial, by an impartial jury of the State and district where in the crime shall have been committed, which district shall have been previously ascertained by law, and to be informed of the nature and case of the accusation; to be confronted with the witnesses against him; to have compulsory process for obtaining witnesses in his faovr, and to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence.
    • A lawyer has a duty of loyalty to their client, regardless of the relative strength of weakness of a client's claim. Stockton v. Ford, 52 U.S. (11 How.) 232 (1850).
    • That duty of loyalty implies that a lawyer has a duty of advocating for their client with utmost devotion and diligence.
    • To fulfill that duty, the lawyer must avoid conflicts of interest.
    • In order for the duty to apply, however, there must be an attorney client relationship. See Rohm and Haas Co. v. Dow Chemical Co., 2009 W.L. 445609 (Del. Ch. 2009).
      • Rohm and Haas Co. v. Dow Chemical Co., 2009 W.L. 445609 (Del. Ch. 2009)
        • Facts
          • Dow was a former client of Wachtell, which represented the company on matters substantially related to the current proceedings. Wachtell, however, now represented the plaintiffs, Rohm and Haas Co.
          • Dow argues that they are currently clients of Wachtell because Wachtell did not take steps to inform Dow that it was no longer a client, following Wachtell's past representation.
          • Given that Dow had provided sensitive information to Wachtell, Dow moved to disqualify Wachtell from representing Rohm and Haas Co.
        • Question
          • Should Wachtell be disqualified because of a conflict of interest?
        • Analysis
          • No. Dow knew that Wachtell was representing Rohm and Haas Co. during the merger agreement and did not object.
          • Why? Because if Dow truly felt that they were a current client of Wachtell, they should not have sat across the table from their own lawyers, and they should have objected then there.
          • Moreover, Dow had obtained its own separate counsel during the merger agreement and did not object.
          • The motion to disqualify is denied.
        • Conclusion
          • For the duty of loyalty to apply, there must be an attorney client relationship.
      • Murray v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co., 583 F.3d 173 (2d Cir. 2009)
        • Facts
          • Plaintiffs Murray et. al. were policyholders of Metropolitan Life Insurance Company ("Metro Life"), back when the company was a mutual insurance company. The company is now called MetLife, Inc. ("MetLife"), the defendants.
          • In 2000, MetroLife demutualized and turned into MetLife.
          • Murray et. al. filed suit, alleging that they had been misled and shortchanged by the company in the demutualization transaction.
          • 9 years after the complaint was filed, and 5 weeks before trial, Murray et. al. moved to disqualify MetLife's counsel, Debevoise & Plimpton LLP ("Debevoise"), alleging that, since Debevoise had been Metro Life's counsel before and during demutualization, they were also Murray et. al.'s counsel during the the demutualization transaction.
          • MetLife responded with the Doctrine of Laches, arguing that, as a matter of law, the policyholders of a mutual insurance company are not a priori the clients of that company's corporate counsel, and charged that Murray et. al.'s motion was made for improper tactical purposes.
        • Question
          • Was Debevoise both the plaintiff and the defendant's counsel at the time of demutualization?
        • Analysis
          • It is well-settled law that a corporation's outside counsel represents the corporation, not its shareholders or other constituents. See Rest. 3d of the Rule Governing Lawyers, § 96 cmt. b.
          • These rules apply as well to a mutual insurance company.
          • Under New York Law, a "mutual insurance company is a cooperative enterprise in which the policyholders constitute the members for whose benefit the company is organized, maintained, and operated.
          • Furthermore, a policyholder is in no sense a partner of the policy-issuing corporation; the policy holder's relationship to the corporation is purely contractual.
          • Finally, not every beneficiary of a lawyer's advice is deemed a client.
            • A lawyer may provide an evaluation of a matter affecting a client for the use of someone other than the client, if the lawyer reasonably believes that doing so is compatible with other aspects of the lawyer's relationship with the client. See N.Y.R. Prof'l. Conduct 2.3(a).
          • The decision of E.D.N.Y. is reversed and remanded.
        • Conclusion
          • The fact that a person is a beneficiary to a lawyer's advice does not imply that that person is a client.
    • A lawyer must not represent a client if the representation involves a concurrent conflict of interest. Model Rule 1.7(a).

    When Is There a Concurrent Conflict of Interest?

    • There is a concurrent conflict of interest if either of the following occur:
      • the representation of one client will be directly adverse to another client; or
      • there is a significant risk that the representation of one or more clients will be materially limited by the lawyer's responsibilities to another client, a former client, or a third person or by a personal interest of the lawyer.
      • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.7(a)(1)-(2).
    Conflicts Resulting in "Materially Limited" Representation
    • If a lawyer's ability to consider, recommend, or carry out an appropriate course of action for a client is limited because of the lawyer's representation of other clients, the representation is "materially limited." See Model Rule 1.7, cmt. 8.
    • For example, if a lawyer represents multiple defendants, and there are substantial conflicts between the positions taken by the defendants, or there are substantially different possibilities of claim settlements or liabilities in question given such representation, there may be a conflict of interest. See Sanford v. Commonwealth of Virginia, 687 F. Supp. 2d 591 (E.D. Va. 2009).
    Conflicts Between the Client's Interests and the Lawyer's Personal or Financial Interests
    • Generally, a lawyer cannot enter into a business transaction with a client or knowingly acquire an ownership, possessory, security, or other pecuniary interests adverse to a client. See Model Rule 1.8(a).
    • However, a lawyer may do so, provided that:
      • the transaction's terms are disclosed and fair and reasonable to the client,
      • the lawyer advises the client in writing of the desirability of independent counsel,
      • the lawyer gives the client a reasonable opportunity to seek independent counsel, and
      • the client gives informed consent, in a writing signed by her, to the transaction's essential terms, the lawyer's role in the transaction, and whether the lawyer is representing the client in the transaction.

    Literary and Media Rights to Portrayals of the Representation

    • Moreover, before the end of a client's representation, a lawyer cannot make or negotiate an agreement giving herself literary or media rights to a portrayal or account based in substantial part on information relating to the representation. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.8(d).
    • Thus, a lawyer may not enter into a media rights contract with a client as a fee arrangement. See Beets v. Collins, 986 F.2d 1478 (5th Cir. 1993).
      • Beets v. Collins, 986 F.2d 1478 (5th Cir. 1993)
        • Facts
          • The plaintiff Beets was represented by the lawyer E. Ray Andrews ("Andrews Sr.") on the matter of murdering her husband.
          • As payment for the representation, Andrews Sr. and Beets entered into a contract transferring all literary and media rights related to her case to Andrews Sr.'s son, E. Ray Andrews, Jr. ("Andrews Jr.").
          • Beets was convicted of murder.
          • On habeas petition, Beets asserted that the media rights contract generated a conflict of interest in violation of her 6th Amendment right to effective assistance of counsel at trial.
          • The district court granted, and the State of Texas appealed.
        • Question
          • Whether a contract granting trial counsel media rights to her client's representation as payment of fees violates the rules of professional conduct, thereby creating a serious potential conflict of interest rendering ineffective assistance of counsel.
        • Analysis
          • Fee arrangements like that presented here have been denounced by legal community.
          • These types of contracts are almost universally considered to be serious potential conflicts of interest.
          • Particularly in a capital murder case, it is impermissible for trial counsel to gain his fee by marketing on the tragedy and misery of his client, her family, and, worst, the victim and his loved ones.
          • But, these contracts are serious potential conflicts of interest, rather than actual conflicts of interest.
          • Thus, to prevail on the claim that a conflict of interest rendered ineffective assistance of counsel claim, the claimant must demonstrate that there was an actual conflict of interest, and that such conflict of interest adversely affected counsel's performance.
          • Beets has failed to demonstrate this requirement.
          • The district court's decision granting the writ of habeas corpus is reversed.
        • Conclusion
          • A lawyer may not enter into a media rights contract as a fee arrangement.
    Conflicts from Sexual Relationships
    • Unless a consensual sexual relationship existed between a lawyer and her client before the client-relationship commenced, a lawyer may not have sexual relations with a client. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.8(j).
    • If the client is an organization, the organization's lawyer may not have a sexual relationship with a constituent of the organization who supervises, directs, or regularly consults with that lawyer concerning the organization's legal matters. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.8(j), cmt. 17.
    Conflicts from the Lawyer being an Advocate-Witness
    • In some situations, a lawyer, in her representation of her client, might take the witness stand to advocate for her client.
    • A lawyer may do so only if one of the following apply:
      • her testimony relates to an uncontested issue;
      • her testimony relates to the nature and value of legal services rendered in the case; or
      • the lawyer's disqualification would result in substantial hardship on the client.
      • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 3.7(a)(1)-(3).
    • If the lawyer does not fall into one of the exceptions above, she may not act as an advocate witness. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 3.7(a).
    Conflicts from Representation Adverse to a Former Client
    • Recall that a lawyer maintains duties to former clients. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.9.
    • A lawyer may not represent a person in the same, or a substantially related, matter of a former client if:
      • the former client's interests are materially adverse to that person, and
      • the former client's information related to representation was obtained by the lawyer and such information is material to the person's matter.
      • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.9(b)(1)-(2).
    • Moreover, in representing a client, a lawyer may not use a former client's information relating to representation to the disadvantage of the former client (except as the rules permit or when the information has become generally known). Cf. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.9(c)(1).
    When is a Former Client's Matter Substantially Related to the Current Client's Matter?
    • Matters are "substantially related" if they involve the same transaction or legal dispute, or if there is a substantial risk that confidential factual information that would have been obtained in the former client's representation would materially advance the current client's position in her matter. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.9, cmt. 3.
      • Westinghouse Elec. Corp. v. Gulf Oil Corp., 588 F.2d 221 (7th Cir. 1978).
      • Facts
        • In 1968, Gulf Oil Corp. ("Gulf") retained the New Mexico law firm Bigbee, Stephenson, Carpenter & Crout ("Bigbee") to represent Gulf in its New Mexico uranium operations.
        • From 1971 to 1976, Bigbee provided Gulf patenting, lease drafting, litigation, resolution counseling, and lobbying services.
        • In 1976, Westinghouse Elec. Corp. ("Westinghouse"), a nuclear reactor manufacturer, filed suit in N.D. Ill. against several corporations, two of which were Gulf and United Nuclear Corporation ("UNC"). Westinghouse alleged that:
          • Gulf and UNC participated in an international cartel that fixed and increased uranium prices in the United States, which in turn led to their defaulting on uranium supply contracts.
        • In response to this litigation, Bigbee represented UNC.
        • In defense, Gulf argued that the largest supply of their uranium, the Mt. Taylor reserves, were simply not in production at the time.
        • Gulf's defense depended on confidential information entrusted to Bigbee.
        • However, while UNC was a co-defendant, it had interests adverse to Gulf; it sought to exculpate itself by inculpating Gulf.
        • In light of this dilemma, Gulf moved to disqualify Bigbee.
        • N.D. Ill. denied the motion, concluding that Bigbee's work for Gulf regarding the Mt. Taylor reserves focused on real estate transactions, rather than the actual amount of untapped and undeveloped uranium reserves.
        • Gulf appealed to the 7th Cir.
      • Question
        • Whether the district court erred in denying the motion to disqualify counsel.
      • Analysis
        • The district court denied the motion to disqualify counsel because there was no substantial relationship between the current matter and a former client's matter.
        • Whether there is a substantial relationship between a current matter and a former matter is determined by asking whether, during the former representation, counsel might have acquired information related to the current matter.
        • To answer this question, the trial judge must perform three operations:
          1. establish a factual reconstruction of the former representation's scope,
          2. from that reconstruction, determine whether confidential information allegedly given would have been given to counsel during that representation, and
          3. determine whether that information is relevant to the issues raised in current litigation pending against the former client.
        • N.D. Ill. erred in applying this framework.
          • Bigbee prepared mining patents and real estate transactions relating to Gulf's uranium properties.
          • It is reasonable to infer that during those representations, Gulf would have given information relating to the actual amount of untapped and undeveloped uranium reserves.
          • This information is unquestionably relevant to the issues raised in the current litigation, as Gulf's defense depends on it.
        • For these reasons, disqualification is required; the decision of N.D. Ill. is reversed.
      • Conclusion
        • To determine whether a current client matter and a former client matter are substantially related, the following operations are performed:
          1. First, determine the former representation's scope through factual reconstruction.
          2. Second, from that reconstruction, determine whether confidential information would have been given to counsel during that representation.
          3. Third, determine whether that confidential information is relevant to the issues raised in current litigation pending against the former client.
        • If the confidential information is relevant and adverse to the former client, counsel cannot represent the current client.
    Conflicts from Former Government Lawyers
    • A former government lawyer is prohibited from using information relating to her representation of the government to the disadvantage of the government, except as the law or the rules otherwise permit. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.11(a)(1); r. 1.9(c).
    • A former government lawyer cannot represent a client in a matter she participated personally and substantially in as a government lawyer, unless the appropriate government agency gives its informed consent, in writing, to the representation. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.11(a)(2).
    • If a former government lawyer is a law firm employee and is disqualified because of the rules above, no lawyers in the law firm may represent a client in such a matter unless:
      • (a) the disqualified lawyer is timely screened from any participation in the matter and takes no part of the representation's fee; and
      • (b) written notice is promptly given to the government agency of the issue to allow it to determine compliance with these rules.
      • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.11(b)(1)–(2).
    • Babineaux v. Foster, 2005 WL 711604 (E.D. La. 2005)
      • Facts
        • In 2003, Tysonia Babineaux was dismissed as the City of Hammond, Lousiana recreation director.
        • Babineaux sued the City of Hammond and Mayor Foster ("the City"), for violations of Civil Rights Act of 1964, Title VII and Louisiana's anti-employment discrimination statute.
        • Babineaux retained Douglas D. Brown as counsel for her employment discrimination claim.
        • Brown had been an assistant city attorney for the City of Hammond during Mayor Foster's predecessor's adminstration, Mayor Louis J. Tallo, which ended in 2002.
        • The City of Hammond moved to disqualify Brown, as Babineaux had filed a complaint in 2001, and the current subject matter is "similar in all respects" to that complaint.
        • Moreover, Brown had knowledge of confidential information relating to the 2001 complaint.
        • Brown argues that there is not conflict of interest, because all of the events giving rise to the current complaint occurred after he left the City of Hammond Attorney's office.
      • Question
        • Whether a former government lawyer may represent a client in a current matter whose events arose after the lawyer left government employment, even if the current matter is substantially similar to a former matter that arose during the lawyer's government employment.
      • Analysis
        • The comments of ABA Model Rule 1.11, on which Lousiana's professional conduct rule is based, states that a former government lawyer is disqualified only from particular matters in which the lawyer participated personally and substantially.
        • Former government lawyers are provided more lenient standards because for governments to gain good, young lawyers, they cannot prevent their lawyers from gaining non-government employment after devoting time to public service.
        • A lawyer has not "personally and substantially participated" in a matter if she is only "tenously and nominally" connected to the matter. See Hernandez v. Johnson, 108 F.3d 554 (5th Cir. 1997).
        • E.D. Wis. disqualified a former Assistant U.S. Attorney from representing a criminal defendant where the attorney (1) was currently defending a client on the same charge that he was previously assigned to prosecute; (2) had received and reviewed investigative reports in connection with the prosecution; and (3) had previously discussed the matter with the head law enforcement agent directing the investigation and arrest. See United States v. Clark, 333 F. Supp. 2d 789 (E.D. Wis. 2004).
        • D.D.C. disqualifed a former FDA attorney from representing a tobacco company after the government produced timesheets indicating that the attorney had logged 382 hours on substantially-related litigation. See United States v. Phillip Morris, Inc., 312 F. Supp. 2d 27 (D.D.C. 2004).
        • Here, Brown's only involvement in the 2001 matter was that he was copied on a letter from Mayor Tallo containing her "confidential" response to Babineaux's complaint. Brown participated in no investigation, and he does not recall receiving the letters, let alone their content.
        • The motion to disqualify is denied.
      • Conclusion
        • Former government lawyers are prohibited from participating in a current matter only if the current matter is one where the former government lawyer participated personally and substantially in.
    Conflicts from Current Government Lawyers
    • The rules of concurrent conflict of interest apply to current government lawyers. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.11(d)(1).
    • A current government lawyer may not use a former client's information relating to representation to the disadvantage of the former client. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.11(d)(1); 1.9(c).
    • Current government lawyers cannot participate in a matter they participated personally and substantially in before government employment, unless the appropriate government agency gives its informed consent, confirmed in writing. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.11(d)(2)(i).
    • Current government lawyers cannot negotiate for private employment with any person who is involved as a party or as a lawyer for a party in a matter she is particating personally and substantially. Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.11(d)(2)(ii).
    Conflicts from Being a Third-Party Neutral
    • A lawyer is a third-party neutral when she assists two or more persons, who are not clients, to resolve a dispute or matter between the persons. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 2.4(a).
    • If a lawyer acts as a third-party neutral, she must inform the parties that she is not representing them. See Model Rules of Pr. Condcut r. 2.4(b).
    • Because a lawyer acting as a third-party neutral does not represent the parties in dispute, attorney-client privilege does not apply to information related to the dispute.
    • A conflict of interest may occur in being a third-party neutral when the lawyer had priorly represented one of the parties in dispute on the matter.
    • A lawyer cannot act as a third-party neutral to a dispute where she formerly represented one of the parties in dispute, unless all parties to the proceeding give informed consent, confirmed in writing. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.12(a).
    • If a law firm lawyer is disqualified from acting as a third-party neutral in a matter where she formerly represented one of the parties in dispute, then no other lawyer in that firm may represent an involved party or act as a third-party neutral unless: (1) the disqualified lawyer is timely screened from participating, and taking no part of the fee in, the matter, and (2) written notice is promptly given to the parties and any appropriate tribunal to ensure the law firm's compliance of (1). Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.12(c)(1)–(2).
    Imputation of Conflicts of Interest
    • If a lawyer is associated with a law firm, and is disqualified from representing a client because of a concurrent conflict of interest (r. 1.7) or because of her duties to a former client (r. 1.9), none of the law firm's lawyers may represent the client unless:
      • the lawyer is disqualified because of a personal interest conflict and does not represent a significant risk of materially limiting representation; or
      • the lawyer is disqualified because the matter is the same or substantially related to a matter from her former firm, and:
        • the disqualified lawyer is timely screened from participating, and taking any fee, in the matter;
        • written notice is promptly given to any former client to enable them to ensure compliance with this rule;
        • such written notice includes:
          • a description of the screening procedures used;
          • a statement of the firm's and the disqualified lawyer's compliance with this rule;
          • a statement that review may be available before a tribunal; and
          • an agreement by the firm to respond promptly to the former client's written inquiries or objections to the screening procedures; and
        • certifications of compliance with this Rule along with the screening procedures are provided to the former client by the screend lawyer and by firm partner, at reasonable intervals between the former client's written request and termination of the screening procedures.
        • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.10(a)(1)–(2).
    • The above rule does not apply, however, if the following are met:
      • the would-be disqualified lawyer has terminated her association with the firm;
      • the matter is not the same or substantially related to that in which the formerly associated lawyer represented their client; and
      • any lawyer remaining in the firm does not have information protected by Rules 1.6 and 1.9(c) that is material to the matter.
      • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.10(b).
    What Constitues Sufficient Screening?
    • In all sufficient screening, the following must be met:
      • The screening must result in the disqualified lawyer being precluded from participating in the disputed matter. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.10(a).
      • The screening must result in the disqualified lawyer being precluded from taking any fee in the disputed matter. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 1.10(a).
      • The screening must be in place either at the time of, or before, the disqualifying event occurred. See LaSalle Nat'l Bank v. County of Lake, 703 F.2d 252 (7th Cir. 1983).
        • LaSalle Nat'l Bank v. County of Lake, 703 F.2d 252 (7th Cir. 1983)
          • Facts
            • Marc Seidler ("Seidler") is a lawyer associated with the Chicago law firm Rudnick & Wolfe ("Rudnick").
            • In 1981, Rudnick, representing LaSale National Bank ("LaSalle"), sued Lake County.
            • Seidler, however, was an Assistant State's Attorney for Lake County before joining Rudnick.
            • Lake County moved to disqualify the Seidler because of the County's relationship to Seidler.
            • The district court granted the motion.
          • Question
            • Is a law firm disqualified from representing a client in a matter where one of their associates was formerly employed by the adverse party?
          • Analysis
            • Seidler is clearly disqualified from representing LaSalle.
            • The remaining question is whether the law firm, Rudnick, is disqualified entirely from representing LaSalle.
            • Knowledge possessed by one attorney in a law firm is presumed to be shared with the firm's other attorneys.
            • But, that presumption may be rebutted by establishing that the "infected" attorney was "screened," or insulated, from all participation in, and information about, a case.
            • If this is successfully established, the law firm is not entirely disqualified based on the prior employment of a member.
            • This rebuttal is permitted because if past government employment results in automatic disqualification of future employers from representing clients adverse to the former government employer, government attorneys would likely become the "Typhoid Marys" of the private sector.
            • Sufficient screening may be done in various ways:
              • Denying the lawyer files, profits, and fees connected with the matter;
              • Prohibiting discussion of the matter in the lawyer's presence;
              • Prohibiting all other lawyers in the firm from showing the lawyer documents related to the matter;
              • Files connected to the matter were kept in a locked cabinet, with its keys held by partners and issued only on a "need to know" basis;
            • However, in all cases were screening was sufficient, the screening procedure was in place either before, or at the time, the disqualifying event occurred.
            • Here, Rudnick's screening procedure was not in place until the disqualification motion was filed.
            • Therefore, there was no sufficient screening, and the entire firm is disqualified from representation.
            • The district court's decision is affirmed.
          • Conclusion
            • Knowledge possessed by a disqualified attorney in a law firm is presumed to be shared with the firm's other attorneys, but that presumption may be rebutted by establishing that the disqualified attorney was sufficiently screened from all participation in, information about, and fees or profits from, the disputed matter.
    • Note, however, that even if sufficient screening is provided, if the disqualified lawyer possesses information related to the opposing party that is material or significant to the matter, disqualification will likely result. See Kassis v. Teacher's Ins. and Annuity Ass'n, 93 N.Y.2d 611 (1999).
      • Kassis v. Teacher's Ins. and Annuity Ass'n, 93 N.Y.2d 611 (1999)
        • Facts
          • Henry Kassis ("Kassis") and North River Insurance Company ("North River") (together the "plaintiffs"), represented by the law firm Weg & Meyers ("Weg"), sued Teacher's Insurance & Annuity Association and Cauldwell-Wingate Company, Inc. ("defendants") for property damage.
          • Defendants were represented by the law firm Thurm & Heller ("Thurm").
          • Plaintiffs moved to disqualify Thurm from continued representation, alleging that one of their lawyers participating in the matter, Charles Arnold ("Arnold"), was a former associate of Weg who had participated in the the current litigation while employed there.
            • At Weg, Arnold assisted Joshua Mallin, the Weg partner in charge of the case. Arnold conducted depositions, attended mediation sessions, appeared as counsel for Kassis at a physical examination of the damaged property, and conversed with Kassis regularly.
            • Upon leaving Weg and joining Thurm, Roula Thoefanis, the partner at Thurm in charge of the current case, cautioned Arnold that would not be participating in the current litigation and was not to discuss the matter with anyone at the firm.
          • The trial court denied plaintiffs' motion, finding that Arnold's involvement in the matter at the time of his Thurm employment was limited.
          • The appellate court affirmed.
        • Question
          • If an attorney associated with a law firm representing one party leaves her law firm and joins the adverse party's retained law firm, is her new law firm completely disqualified from representing their client?
        • Analysis
          • If an attorney is disqualified from representation because of acquiring confidential client information at a former law firm, the presumption that the entirety of the attorney's current law firm must be disqualified may be rebutted.
          • To successfully rebut the presumption, it must be established that information acquired by the disqualified lawyer is unlikely to be significant or material to the litigation.
          • Whether the disqualified attorney possesses significant or material information is determined by assessing the case's facts.
            • If the disqualified attorney formerly worked in a small law firm characterized by informality and conductive to "constant cross-pollination" and a "cross current of discussion and ideas" among all of the firm's attorneys on all matters, there is a greater likelihood of acquiring material client confidences.
            • If the disqualified attorney formerly worked in a law firm whose offices are not physically isolated and files are made open and available to all lawyers, there will be a greater likelihood of acquiring material client confidences.
          • Additionally, to successfully rebut the presumption, the law firm must provide adequate screening measures to separate the disqualified lawyer and eliminate any involvement by that lawyer in the representation.
          • Here, Arnold played an appreciable role in the current matter while at Weg, and possessed information material or signficant to the current matter.
          • Failing to meet this first requirement, it is inconsequential that Thurm erected screening procedures.
          • The appellate court's decision is reversed, and disqualification is required.
        • Conclusion
          • If an attorney associated with a law firm representing one party leaves her law firm and joins the adverse party's retained law firm, the adverse party's retained law firm is presumed to be disqualified from continuing representation. However, that presumption may be rebutted, but only if: (a) the law firm establishes that the disqualified attorney does not possess information material or significant to the current matter, and (b) the law firm provides adequate screening measures preventing the disqualified lawyer from any involvement in the representation.

    Exceptions to the Conflict of Interest Prohibition

    • Under Model Rule 7.1(b), even if there is a conflict of interest, a lawyer may represent a client only if the following requirements are met:
      • the lawyer reasonably believes she will be able to provide competent and diligent representation to each affected client;
      • the representation is not prohibited by law;
      • the representation does not involve the assertion of a claim by one client against another client represented by the lawyer in the same litigation or other proceeding before a tribunal; and
      • each affected client gives informed consent in writing.
    • Conflict of Interest Checklist
      • There is a conflict of interest when either of the following is present:
        • The representation of one client will be directly adverse to another client.
        • There is a significant risk that the representation of one or more clients will be materially limited by the lawyer's responsibilities to:
          • another client;
          • a former client;
          • a third person; or
          • a personal interest of the lawyer.
      • But, despite the conflict of interest, the lawyer may still provide representation, only if all of the following are met:
        • the lawyer reasonably believes that she will be able to provide competent and diligent representation to each affected client;
        • the representation is not prohibited by law;
        • the representation does not involve the assertion of a claim by one client against another client represented by the lawyer in the same litigation or other proceeding before a tribunal; and
        • each affected client gives informed consent, confirmed in writing.
    "Reasonably Believes" and "Competent Diligent Representation"
    • A lawyer may represent a client despite the existence of a concurrent conflict of interest only if the lawyer "reasonably believes" that she will be able to provide "competent" and "diligent" representation to each client. Model Rule 1.7(b)(1).
    • To provide "competent" representation, the lawyer must employ the legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation that are reasonably necessary for the representation. Model Rule 1.1.
    • To provide "diligent" representation, the lawyer must act with "reasonable diligence and promptness in representating a client. Model Rule 1.3.
    • If the lawyer representats multiple defendants, and there are substantial conflicts between the positions taken by the defendants, or there are substantially different possibilities of the claim settlements or liabilities in question, the lawyer may fail to provide "competent diligent represetation," even if she reasonably believes that she can do so. See Sanford v. Commonwealth of Virginia, 687 F.Supp.2d 591 (E.D. Va. 2009).
      • Sanford v. Commonwealth of Virginia, 687 F. Supp. 2d 591 (E.D. Va. 2009)
        • Facts
          • Plaintiff's decedent, John Sanford, underwent a surgery at a Virgina-run hospital.
          • Two days after his surgery, Sanford grew delirious, acting belligerently.
          • Virginia police ("VCUPD") were summoned to restrain him.
          • The police officers wrestled Sanford to the ground, handcuffed him, and held him down for about 30 minutes, and was subsequently injected with a sedative.
          • When VCUPD turned Sanford over, he was dead.
          • Sanford's family sued VCUPD and the hospital staff ("MCV") for gross negligence, battery, false imprisonment, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and 4th and 14th Amendment violations.
          • The same lawyer represented VCUPD, and another lawyer represented the MCV defendants.
          • Sanford moved to disqualify the lawers for operating under a conflict of interest.
            • Sanford argued that VCUPD's counsel could not represent all of the VCUPD plaintiffs.
              • First, there was a conflict of interest between Colonel Fuller (the officers' supervisor) and the officers themselves. The officers claimed that there was no training for the situations like the decedents, while Colonel testified to the contrary.
              • Second, there was a conflict of interest between the officers who first arrived on scene, and the officers who arrived later. This is because it is VCUPD's protocol that the first responding officer leads, and that subsequent responding officers follow that officer's instructions.
            • Sanford then argued that MCV's counsel could not represent all of the MCV plaintiffs.
              • First, there is a conflict of interest between Dr. Meguid, an MCV plaintiff, and the other MCV plaintiffs, because some of counsel's expert witnesses will provide testimony against Dr. Meguid's assertions.
              • Second, there is an additional conflict of interest between Dr. Meguid and the other plaintiffs, because Dr. Meguid cautioned against using the sedative, but the other plaintiffs did so anyway, asserting that they were unaware of such precautions.
        • Question
          • Whether there were a conflicts of interest among VCUPD and MCV's counsel.
        • Analysis
          • A lawyer must not represent a client if the representation involves a concurrent conflict of interest. Va. R. Pro. Conduct 1.7.
          • There is a concurrent conflict of interest if "there is a significant risk that the representation of one or more clients will be materially limited by the lawyer's responsibilities to another client. Id.
          • Furthermore, there may be a concurrent conflict of interest if there is a substantial discrepancy in the parties' testimony, incompatibility in positions in relation to an opposing party, or the fact that there are substantially different possibilities of settlement of the claims or liabilities in question. Id.
          • Here, there are clear conflicts of interests between the positions the defendants have taken.
        • Conclusion
          • If there is a substantial discrepancy in the in the parties' testimony, incompatibility in positions in relation to an opposing party, or the fact that there are substantially different possibilities of settlement of the claims or liabilities in question, there may be a concurrent conflict of interest.
    "Not Prohibited by Law"
    • For a lawyer to represent a client despite a conflict of interest, the representation must not be prohibited by law. Model Rule 1.7(b)(2).
    • Because the requirements of Rule 1.7(b)(2) are all inclusive, clients cannot consent to a representation containing a conflict of interest if such representation is prohibited by law. See Model Rule 1.7(b).
    "Assertion of a Claim by One Client Against Another"
    • A lawyer cannot represent opposing parties in the same litigation, regardless of consent. Model Rule 1.7(b)(3).
    • Per Rule 1.7 cmt. 17, the "institutional interest in vigorous development of each client's position" negates consent when the clients are "aligned directly against each other in the same litigation or other proceeding before a tribunal."
    "Informed Consent, Confirmed in Writing"
    • For a lawyer to represent a client despite a conflict of interest, the lawyer must obtain informed consent, in writing, from each affected client. Model Rule 1.7(b)(4).
    What Is Informed Consent?
    • To have informed consent, each affected client must be aware of the relevant circumstances and the reasonably foreseeable consequences of representation with a conflict of interest. See Model Rule 1.7 cmt. 17.
    How Is the "Consent Confirmed in Writing" Requirement Met?
    • Consent is "confirmed in writing" if the consent is stated in a document. See Model Rule 1.7 cmt. 20.
    • Even if consent is confirmed in writing, the lawyer must still meet with the client and explain the risks and advantages of representation, the possible alternatives, as well as affording the client a reasonable opportunity to consider such discussions. See Model Rule 1.7 cmt. 20.
    Revoking Consent
    • A client is free to revoke consent to a representation despite a concurrent conflict of interest at any time. See Model Rule 1.7(b), cmt. 21.
    Can a Lawyer Obtain Consent to Waive Future Conflicts?
    • Lawyers are permitted to request a client's consent to waive future conflicts, but only to the extent that the client reasonably understands the consequences of such a waiver. See Model Rule 1.7, cmt. 22.
    • Thus, for a consent to waive future conflicts to be effective, the waiver must be specific to the circumstances, and the lawyer must provide the client a comprehensive explanation of the future representations that might arise as well as their reasonably foreseeable consequences. See Model Rule 1.7, cmt. 22.
    • A lawyer is free to enter into an agreement wherein the client consents to all current and future conflicts of interest, but such consent is only effective, and the agreement only enforceable, if the lawyer discloses to the client all the facts and circumstances that may affect the client's interests. See Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton, LLP v. J-M Manufacturing Co., 6 Cal. 5th (2019).
      • Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton, LLP v. J-M Manufacturing Co., 6 Cal. 5th 59 (2019)
        • Facts
          • Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton, LLP ("Sheppard") represented J-M Manufacturing Co. ("J-M") in a federal qui tam action, brought on behalf of several public entities, one of which includes South Tahoe Public Utility District ("South Tahoe").
            • Note: A qui tam action (also called a "popular action") is a lawsuit where a private party, called a relator, files a lawsuit on the government's behalf. The government, not the relator, is the real plaintiff, and if the government succeeds, the relator receives a share of the award.
          • J-M and South Tahoe entered into an agreements with Sheppard consenting to waive all current and future conflicts of interest, but the agreements did not refer to a specific conflict.
          • Sheppard, however, was also representing South Tahoe on certain employment matters.
          • Upon discovering Sheppard's concurrent representation, South Tahoe moved to disqualify Sheppard from the action. The motion was granted.
          • After the lawsuit concluded, J-M sought a disgorgement of Sheppard's fees. The dispute which was sent to arbitration.
          • The arbitrators ruled that Sheppard's failure to disclose its concurrent was not so egregious or serious to warrant a disgorgement of fees.
          • The superior court confirmed the decision, but the Court of Appeal reversed, finding that Sheppard's undisclosed conflict of interest violated the rules of professional conduct, and as such, nullified the parties' consent agreement, including its arbitration clause.
        • Question
          • Whether an undisclosed conflict of interest nullifies an agreement consenting to all future conflicts of interest.
        • Analysis
          • Under the California Rules of Professional Conduct, a lawyer cannot, without the informed written consent of each client, represent a client in a matter and at the same time, in a separate matter, accept another client whose interest in the first matter is adverse to the client in the first matter. Cal. R. Prof. Conduct 3-310(C)(3).
          • At the time Sheppard entered into the the conflict waiver agreement, Sheppard had an existing conflict of interest by representing both J-M and South Tahoe.
          • Further, Sheppard knew of this conflict of interest, but did not inform either J-M or South Tahoe of its existence. As such, neither J-M nor South Tahoe gave "informed" consent.
          • Additionally, upon its discovery of the conflict, South Tahoe revoked is consent, rendering the conflict waiver agreement unenforceable.
          • The issue, however, is that the conflict waiver agreement covered both future and current conflicts of interest.
          • The question then, is, whether the conflict waiver agreement consituted effective consent.
          • The purpose of requiring effective consent is to enforce the attorney's duty of loyalty.
          • Following this purpose, for a client to give effective consent, her counsel must disclose all material facts, which include existing conflicts of interest.
          • Thus, by failing to disclose to J-M of a current conflict of interest, Sheppard failed to disclose to its client all material facts.
          • The Court of Appeal's judgment is affirmed, insofar as it reversed the superior court's judgment regarding the arbitration award. The Court of Appeal's judgment ordering disgorgement of all fees collected is reversed and remanded.
        • Conclusion
          • For a client's consent to waive all current and future conflicts of interest to be effective, client's counsel must disclose all material facts and circumstances that may affect the client's interests.

    The Lawyer's Duties to Tribunals

    Introduction

    • Lawyers are officers of the court, and as such, owe duties both their clients and the judiciary.
    • Because of a lawyers duties to the justice system, there are limits on representation.
    • To remain within those limits, the lawyer must:
      • avoid identifying too closely with their clients; and
      • communicate openly and fully with their clients about the limitations of representation.
    • This section explores the limitations of representation because of a lawyer's duties to the judiciary.
    Not All Lawsuits Can Be Filed
    • A lawyer is prohibited from bringing or defending a proceeding with no basis in law and fact. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 3.1.
    • This rule is supported by FRCP 11, which requires that all papers filed with a court must be signed by the lawyer, certifying that the lawsuit is based in law and fact (i.e., "nonfrivolous"). See Fed. R. Civ. P. 11.
    • If a lawyer violates FRCP 11, sanctions are imposed. Id.
      • When Is a Lawsuit Nonfrivolous?
      • To be nonfrivolous, in bringing the lawsuit, the lawyer must be able to make good faith arguments in support of their clients' positions. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 3.1, cmt. 2.
    Not All Evidence Can Be Presented in Court
    • Once a lawyer has filed a case in court, the lawyer:
      • must not undermine the adjudicative process's integrity; and
      • must knowingly present false testimony; and
      • if false testimony is presented, must correct such testimony.
      • Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 3.3, cmt. 2.
    • Furthermore, if the lawyer knows of legal authority directly adverse to her client's position, she must disclose such authority to the court if not already already disclosed by opposing counsel. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 3.3(a)(2).
    • This means that a legal argument based on a knowingly false representation of law constitutes dishonesty toward a tribunal. See Model Rules of Pr. Conduct r. 3.3, cmt. 4.
    Duty of Candor
    • These rules imply a duty of candor, which implies the following restrictions on representation:
      • A lawyer must not knowingly make a false statement of fact or law to a tribunal. Model Rule 3.3(a)(1).
      • If a lawyer makes a false statement of fact or law, she must correct it. Id.
      • A lawyer must not offer false evidence. Model Rule 3.3(a)(3).
      • If a lawyer, the lawyer's client, or a witness for the client presents false evidence, the lawyer must disclose it to the tribunal and correct it. Id.
    Duty of Rectification
    • They also imply a duty of rectification, which implies the following restrictions on representation:
      • If a lawyer knows that her client has, is, or will engage(d) in criminal or fraudulent conduct, the lawyer must take remedial measures, and, if necessary, disclose such knowledge to the tribunal. Model Rule 3.3(b).

      What Constitutes Fraudulent Conduct?

      • Fraudulent conduct includes doing any of the following:
        • bribing, intimidating, or unlawfully communicating with, a participant in a proceeding;
        • unlawfully destroying or concealing documents or other evidence;
        • failing to disclose information to the tribunal when legally required.
        • Model Rule 3.3, cmt. 12.

      What Constitutes Remedial Measures?

      • A lawyer takes remedial measures if she does the following:
        • The lawyer speaks with the client, advising him of her duty of candor, and seeking his cooperation with withdrawing or correcting false statements or evidence. Model Rule 3.3, cmt. 10.
        • If that fails, the lawyer must withdraw from representation, but only if it is permitted and will undo the effect of the false statement or evidence. Id.
        • If the lawyer cannot withdraw from represenation, she must disclose the client's actions to the tribunal as is reasonably necessary to remedy the situation, even if doing so requires the lawyer to reveal confidential information. Id.
      • Once a disclosure is made, the rest of the situation is left to the tribunal to decide. Id.

    Limitations on the Duties

    • Note that the rules regarding false testimony apply only if the lawyer knows the testimony to be false. A lawyer's reasonable belief that evidence is false does not prohibit her from presenting to a tribunal. Model Rule 3.3, cmt. 7.
    • A lawyer's knowledge, however, can be inferred from the circumstances. See Model Rule 1.0(f).
    • Thus, if, and only if, a lawyer reasonably believes that such testimony might not be false, the lawyer is permitted to present such testimony, and, if the client wishes to present such testimony, must respect the client's wishes. Model Rule 3.3, cmt. 9.
    Not All Arguments Can Be Made in Court

    There are restrictions on what arguments a lawyer may make in court. These restrictions ensure that jurors and other court participants are not misled.

    Rule 3.4(e) - Irrelevant & Unsupported Material; Personal Knowledge & Opinions
    • At trial, a lawyer must not:
      • allude to material that:
        • she does not believe to be relevant; or
        • will not be supported by admissible evidence;
      • assert personal knowledge of disputed facts when testifying a witness;
      • state a personal opinion about:
        • the justness of a cause;
        • a witness's credibility;
        • a civil litigant's culpability;
        • an accused's guilt or innocence.
      • Model Rule 3.4(e).
    Closing Arguments
    • Both prosecutors and defense attorneys must not state their personal opinions about a defendant's guilt. See United States v. Young, 470 U.S. 1 (1985).
    Limits on Gaining & Controlling Access to Witnesses

    Although lawyers have power to limit an adverse party's access to their witnesses, that power is not unlimited. Limits are imposed to ensure that the court is not obstructed from obtaining the truth.

    Rule 3.4 - Fairness to Opposing Party & Counsel
    • A lawyer must not:
      • unlawfully obstruct another party's access to evidence;
      • unlawfully alter, destroy, or conceal material with potential evidentiary value;
      • falsify evidence;
      • counsel, induce, or assist another to perform any of the above;
      • Model Rule 3.4(a)–(b).
    • A lawyer must not request anyone other than the client to refrain from voluntarily giving relevant information to another party, unless:
      • the person is the client's relative, employee, or agent; AND
      • the lawyer reasonably believes that the person's interests are not adversely affected by complying with the request.
      • Model Rule 3.4(f)(1)–(2).
    Paying Witnesses
    • A lawyer must not offer or pay a witness any amount:
      • beyond the witness's reasonable expenses for time spent providing evidence;
      • contingent on the the content of the witness's testimony;
      • contingent on the litigation's outcome; or
      • prohibited by law.
      • See Rest. § 117(a).
    • A lawyer, however, is permitted to offer and pay an expert witness a fee beyond reasonable expenses, but only if the fee is noncontingent. Id.

      • What Constitutes "Reasonable Expenses?"

      • If the witness is a salaried employee, "reasonable expenses" is determined according to lost income. See ABA Formal Op. 96-402 (1996).
      • If the witness is self-employed, "reasonable expenses" may be determined according to the witness's hourly rate. Id.
      • If the witness is retired or unemployed, "reasonable expenses" must be determiend according all relevant circumstances. Id.
    Limits on Communications with Judges and Jurors

    While lawyers are expected to be persuasive advocates, there are limits on what they can do to meet the expectation. These limits are imposed to ensure a fair and accurate proceeding.

    Rule 3.5(a) - Illlicitly Influencing a Litigation Participant
    • A lawyer must not seek to influence a judge, juror, prospective juror, or other official by illegal means. Model Rule 3.5(a).
    • Examples of such illegal means including bribing or threatening; violations of federal and state criminal statutes.
    Rule 3.5(b) - Ex Parte Communications
    • A lawyer must not communicate ex parte (communicating without opposing counsel present) with a judge, juror, prospective juror, or other official during a proceeding unless authorized by law or court order. Model Rule 3.5(b).
    • This rule, however, does not apply to routine communications for scheduling a hearing, obtaining a temporary restraining order, or other similar procedures. See Rest. § 113, cmts. c, d.
    Nonadjudicative Proceedings

    The restrictions on representation do not just apply to adjudicative proceedings (e.g., a court or arbitration proceeding). They also extend to nonadjudicative bodies, such as legislatures, municipal councils, government agencies. Model Rule 3.9.

    Rule 3.9, cmt. 1 - Representations Made to a Nonadjudicative Body
    • If a lawyer represents a client before a legislative body or administrative agency in a nonadjudicative proceeding, she must disclose to the body that she is acting in a representative capacity. Model Rule 3.9.
    Boasting About Connections to the Judiciary

    Lawyers must not state or imply that they have, or are capable of, improper influence.

    Rule 8.4(e) - Boasting About Connections
    • A lawyer must not state or imply an ability to improperly influence a government agency or official, or to achieve results in violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or the law. Model Rule 8.4(e).
    A Lawyer's Actions During a Highly-Publicized Trial

    When a case involves gruesome criminal acts, celebrities, or famous corporations, a lawyer may be tempted to try their case in the court of public opinion.

    Rule 3.6(b) - Trying a Case in the Court of Public Opinion
    • A lawyer participating, or who has participated, in a matter's investigation or litigation must not make extrajudicial (out of court) statements she knows, or reasonably should know, will be made public and have substantial likelihood of materially prejudicing the matter's proceeding. See Model Rule 3.6(b).
    • There are, however, exceptions to this rule.

      • Exceptions

    • Unless otherwise prohibited by law, a lawyer may make an extrajudicial statement containing:
      • the claim, offense, or defense of the persons involved;
      • the identity of the persons involved;
      • information in public record;
      • the fact that an investigation is in progress;
      • the result of any step in litigation;
      • a request for assitance in obtaining evidence and information;
      • a warning of danger about an involved person's behavior (but only if the lawyer reasonably believes an individual or the public interest will likely be substantially harmed); and
      • in a criminal case:
        • the accused's identity, residence, occupation, and family status;
        • if the accused has not been apprehended, necessary information for their apprehension;
        • the fact, time and place or arrest;
        • the identity of investigating and arresting officers or agencies;
        • the length of an investigation.
        • Model Rule 3.6(b)(1)–7.
    Rule 3.6(c) - The Right to Defend a Client in the Court of Public Opinion
    • Under the fair reply provision, a lawyer may make a statement to protect a client from the prejudicial effect of adverse publicity.Model Rule 3.6(c).
    • This rule applies, however, only if the following are met:
      • A reasonable lawyer would believe that remedying the adverse publicity's prejudicial effect requires making the statement.
      • The adverse publicity was not initiated by the lawyer or her client.
      • The statement is limited to information necessary to mitigate the prejudicial effect.
      • The adverse publicity must have, or has a substantial likelihood of having, material prejudice.
      • Model Rule 3.6(c).

    When Does Adverse Publicity Have a Substantial Likelihood of Material Prejudice?

    • Statements about the following subjects likely cause adverse publicity with a substantial likelihood of material prejudice:
      • a party, suspect, or witness's character, credibility, reputation, or criminal record;
      • a witness's identity;
      • a party or witness's expected testimony;
      • the performance or results of an examination or test;
      • a person's refusal or failre to submit to an examination or test;
      • the identity or nature of physical evidence expected to be presented;
      • information the lawyer reasonably should know is likely inadmissible in trial;
      • inadmissible information that the lawyer reasonably should know creates a substantial risk of prejudice;
      • in a criminal case:
        • without explaining that a charge is merely an accusation and that the defendant is presumed innocent, the fact that the defendant has been charged;
        • that the proceeding could result in incarceration;
        • the possibility of a guilty plea;
        • a defendant or suspect's confession, admission, or statement;
        • a defendant or suspect's refusal to make a statement; and
        • an opinion on the defendant or suspect's guilt or innocence in a proceeding that could result in incarceration.
      • See Model Rule 3.6, cmt. 5.
    Rule 3.8 - Prosecutors & Trial Publicity
    • A prosecutor must not make extrajudicial comments with a substantial likelihood of increasing the accused's public condemnation, and must exercise reasonably care in ensuring that investigators, law enforcement personnel, employees, or other persons associated with with prosecutor do not make such comments. Model Rule 3.8(f).
    • A defendant, however, is not entitled to an indcitment's dismissal merely because a prosecutor's extrajudicial comments violated the rules of professional conduct. See United States v. Silver, 103 F.Supp.3d 370 (S.D.N.Y. 2015).\
      • United States v. Silver, 103 F.Supp.3d 370 (S.D.N.Y. 2015)

        Facts

        • In 2015, the United States filed a criminal complaint against Silver, then-speaker of the New York Assembly, for fraud, conspiracy, and extortion, gaining national publicity.
        • Warrant issued, Silver was arrested.
        • After posting bail, Silver issued a short press statement, and later that day, the U.S. attorney for S.D.N.Y. did the same, commenting that the central allegation to the case was that "Silver successfully sought ways to monetize his public office and that he did so in violation of federal law."
        • The U.S. attorney went further, issuing a press release, commenting on a connection between Silver's charges and a broader "culture of corruption."
        • The U.S. attorney then went on Twitter, stating that Silver monetized on his position as speaker.
        • Then even more: the U.S. attorney went on MSNBC and made further comments about the problems caused by corruption.
        • A grand jury then returned with an indictment charging Silver with fraud and extortion.
        • Silver filed a motion claiming that, through his conduct, the U.S. attorney caused prejudice, and as remedy, the Court must dismiss the indictment or poll the grand jury and disclose its minutes.

        Question

        • Whether the prosecutor's extrajudicial comments were (a) sufficently prejudicial to overcome a grand jury proceeding's presumption of regularity, and (b) warrant the extreme sanction of dismissing the indictment or polling the grand jury and disclosing its minutes.

        Analysis

        • Dismissal of an indictment because of a defect in the grand jury proceedings is a drastic remedy rarely used.
        • A prosecutor's violation of disciplinary or ethical rules alone is insufficient to dismiss an indictment.
        • The dismissal of an indictment for non-constitutional error may be appropriate if (a) the violation substantially influenced the grand jury's decision to indict, or (b) there is "grave doubt" that the decision was free from the violation's substantial influence.
        • Here, there was already substantial publicity surrounding Silver before the prosecutor even made the comments.
        • If the prosecutor leaked stories to the media, they were only as prejudicial as the publicity already in place.
        • The prosecutor's comments, however, were inappropriate.
        • But, inappropriate they may be, they are insufficient for dismissing an indictment.
        • Furthermore, Silver has failed to demonstrate a particularized need that outweighs the grand jury proceeding's presumption of secrecy.
        • For these reasons, Silver's motion is denied.
    Defense Counsel & Trial Publicity
    • If, pursuant to a professional conduct rule, a court orders either party to cease making extrajudicial comments, and a party continues, then the party is subject to criminal contempt. See United States v. Cutler, 58 F.3d 825 (2d. Cir. 1995).
      • United States v. Cutler, 58 F.3d 825 (2d Cir. 1995)

        Facts

        • In December 11, 1990, mob boss John Gotti was arrested and charged.
        • Andrew Maloney, the U.S. attorney assigned to the matter, publicly stated that Gotti was a "murderer" and boasted about the government's evidence.
        • Bruce Cutler, Gotti's lawyer, waged a publicity campaign, denying that Gotti was a mob boss.
        • E.D.N.Y. found both attorneys' extrajudicial statements in violation of Local Rule 7, which prohibits certain extrajudicial statements.
        • Despite this finding, Cutler continued the publicity campaign.
        • E.D.N.Y. found Cutler guilty of criminal contempt, and sentenced him to 90 days house arrest, 3 years probation, and suspended his practice license in E.D.N.Y. for 180 days.
        • Cutler appealed to the 2d Cir., arguing that Local Rule 7 was unconstitutional under the 1st Amendment.

        Question

        • Whether Local Rule 7, prohibiting certain extrajudicial statements, is unconstitutional under the 1st Amendment.

        Analysis

        • To hold a defendant in criminal contempt, the government must prove beyond reasonable doubt that:
          1. the court entered a reasonably specific order;
          2. the defendant knew of that order;
          3. the defendant violated that order; and
          4. the defendant's violation was willful.
        • Here, Cutler was on notice to follow definite and specific orders by the judge, so the first two requirements are met.
        • For the third requirement, Cutler argues that the:
          • even if his comments were prohibited by Rule 7, they are prohibited only they are reasonably likely to prejudice the proceedings, and
          • none of the comments he made were reasonably likely to prejudice the proceedings.
        • The Court rejects the second argument, but considers the first.
        • A rule prohibiting certain types of speech from an attorney is valid only if the speech is reasonably likely to interfere with a fair trial or otherwise prejudice the administration of justice.
        • Furthermore, there is a strong, but rebuttable, presumption that speech falling within the categories of Local Rule 7 are reasonably likely to interfere with a fair trial or otherwise prejudice the administration of justice.
        • Here, the district court made findings of fact under the assumption that the reasonable likelihood standard applied. For this reason, the Court will not disturb the district court's judgment.
        • Therfore, the third requirement is met.
        • With respect to the fourth requirement, Cutler argues:
          • he did not know the comments listed in the order to show cause were reasonably likely to prejudice the proceedings.
        • Attorneys are held to a higher standard than lay persons, and his persistent attempts to try Gotti's case in the court of public opinion, despite the judge's warnings, belie any notion that he did not intend to disregard the orders.
        • The district court's judgment is affirmed.
    Obedience to Court Orders
    • A lawyer must not engage in conduct intended to disrupt a tribunal. Model Rule 3.5(d).
    • This includes controlling their witnesses; an attorney's complicity in her witness's violation of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure is sufficient for sanctions. See GMAC Bank v. HTFC Corp., 248 F.R.D. 182 (E.D. Penn. 2008).
      • GMAC Bank v. HTFC Corp., 248 F.R.D. 182 (E.D. Penn. 2008)

        Facts

        • GMAC Bank, a mortgage company, suied HTFC Corp. for breach of contract.
        • During discovery, GMAC's counsel deposed Aaron Wider, owner and CEO of HTFC.
        • GMAC could not complete the Wider's depositions, because of his abusive conduct towards GMAC's counsel, obstructing and delaying the deposition, and failing or evading questions.
        • GMAC moved to compelt Wider's deposition and for sanctions against HTFC's counsel, Joseph Ziccardi.

        Question

        • What is an attorney's duty when she is confronted by her witness's uncivil conduct at deposition?

        Analysis

        • To prevail on a motion to compel, the deponent must either: fail to answer a question, give incomplete answers to a question, or evade answering a question. Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(a)(3)(B)(i), (a)(4), (a)(5)(A).
        • If the motion is granted, the court must: (1) give the nonmovant an opportunity to be heard, and (2) require the nonmovant, whether party or attorney or both, to pay the movant's reasonable expenses for making the motion, including attorney's fees. Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(a)(5)(A).
        • Thus, IF an attorney:
          • improperly advises a deponent to provide evasive or incomplete answers, OR
          • improperly advises a deponent to refuse to answer questions at a deposition, OR
          • engages in conduct that "impedes, delays, or frustates the fair examination of the deponent;
          • THEN the attorney is subject to sanctions.
        • Here, Ziccardi persistently failed to intercede and correct Widener's violations of the federal rules.
        • Ziccardi sat back and allowed Widener to continue his conduct.
        • Ziccardi, through his complicity, violated the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
        • The motion to compel is granted, and sanctions are imposed on both Wider and Ziccardi, in the amount of $29,322.61.
    • A judge, however, must not hold an attorney in contempt for disobeying a court order, if obeying the court order would result in the attorney providing ineffective assistance of counsel See State v. Jones, 2008-Ohio-6994 (Ohio Ct. App. 2008).
      • State v. Jones, 2008-Ohio-6994 (Ohio Ct. App. 2008)

        Facts

        • On August 15, 2007, Jones, a public defender, was appointed to represent Jordan Scott on a misdemeanor assault charge.
        • Scott's trial was set for the following the day.
        • On the morning of the trial, Jones spoke with Scott for twenty minutes, and voiced concerns to the judge that he would not be effective as defense counsel because he needed more time to interview witnesses.
        • The judge replied that the witnesses were present, and that trial would proceed after lunch.
        • After lunch, the trial court reconvened, and trial proceeded.
        • Scott attempted to raise a pretrial motion, and the judge asked if he was ready to start trial.
        • Scott replied no, because he had no opportunity to interview witnesses, to which the judge warned him that if Scott did not proceed with trial, he would find him in contempt of court.
        • Scott refused to proceed, and the judge held him in contempt and ordered him into custody.

        Question

        • If a defense counsel, concerned with the potential of providing ineffective assistance of counsel in proceeding with trial, disobeys the trial judge's order to proceed with trial, may she be held in criminal contempt?

        Analysis

        • Contempt is an act or omissions that substantially disrupts the judicial process in a case (e.g., disobeying a court order, disrespecting the administration of justice, or embarrassing, impeding, or obstructing a court performing its functions).
        • Reviewing a finding of contempt, the standard is: abuse of discretion.
        • An error of law alone is insufficient to establish abuse of discretion; the trial court's decision must imply an unreasonable, arbitrary, or unconscionable attitude.
        • Under Ohio's Code of Professional Conduct, defense attorneys should not be required to violate their duties to their client. Thus, a trial court may commit an error if it holds defense counsel in contempt for refusing to proceed (i.e., disobeying its order denying continuance), while defense counsel fears that proceeding would result in ineffective assistance.
        • Here, the facts show that a continuance was warranted.
          • While defense counsel did not request a specific time extension, completely denying continuance was an abuse of discretion.
          • Any inconvenience to the judiciary for granting a continuance would have been minimal compared to the judge's proposal that this court reverse his decision.
          • Furthermore, defense counsel had legitimate reasons for requesting continuance—he only had two hours to study the facts, the witnesses, and his client, before preparing and constructing a defense.
        • The trial judge's order holding defense counsel in contempt is reversed.

    Duties to Parties Other than the Client

    Duties to Opposing & Third Parties
    • While a lawyer represents a client, she must not (a) knowingly make a false statement of material fact or law to a third person, or (b) if disclosure of a material fact is necessary to avoid assisting a client's criminal or fraudulent act and such disclosure is not prohibited by Rule 1.6, fail to disclose such fact. Model Rule 4.1(a)–(b).
    • A misrepresentation can occur if the lawyer incorporates or affirms another person's statement and the lawyer knows the statement is false. Model Rule 4.1, cmt. 1.
    • A misrepresentation can also occur if the lawyer incorporates or affirms another person's statement and the lawyer knows such statement is only partially true, misleading, or consists of omissions. Id.
    Inadvertent Disclosures
    • If a lawyer receives a document or electronically stored information relating to her client's representation, and knows or reasonably should know that the document or information was inadvertently sent, then the lawyer must promptly notify the sender. Model Rule 4.4(b).
    Communicating with a Represented Party: The No Contact Rule
    • While representing a client, a lawyer must not communicate about the representation with a person the lawyer knows is represented by another lawyer in the matter, unless the lawyer has the other lawyer's consent or is authorized to do so by law or order. Model Rule 4.2.
    Unrepresented Persons
    • While a lawyer communicates with an unrepresented person on behalf of a client, they lawyer (a) must not state or imply that she is disinterested, and (b) if the lawyer knows or reasonably should know that the unrepresented person misunderstands the lawyer's role in the matter, must make reasoanble efforts to correct the misunderstanding. Model Rule 4.3.
    • While a lawyer communicates with an unrepresented person, if the lawyer knows or reasonably should know that the person's interests have a reasonable possibility of, or are in, conflict with her client's interests, she must not give the person legal advice other than securing counsel. Model Rule 4.3.

    Duties to the Law and the Legal Profession

    In representing a client, a lawyer must be mindful of her duties to obey the law and her duties to the legal profession. These duties can trump the lawyer's duties to her client.

    Obedience to Law

    • A lawyer is subject to liability to a client or nonclient just as a nonlawyer would be in similar circumstances. Rest. § 56.
    • But, a lawyer, like other agents, is not liable for the client's acts that make the client liable. Rest. § 56.
    • While a lawyer represents a client, the lawyer must not use means that have no substantial purpose other than to embarrass, delay, or burden a third person, or use methods of obtaining evidence that violate a third person's legal rights. Model Rule 4.4(a).
    • While a lawyer represents a client in a private civil litigation, the lawyer may use the possibility of presenting criminal charges to gain leverage but only if:
      • the criminal matter is related to the client's civil claim,
      • the lawyer has a well-founded belief that both civil and criminal claims are supported by the facts and the law, and
      • the lawyer neither suggests that he has nor seeks to have improper influence over the criminal process.
      • ABA Formal Op. 92-363 (1992).
    • Note, however, that a lawyer's ability to threaten criminal prosecution is limited by criminal laws against extortion and compounding a crime.
    • There are also further constraints on that ability from the following rules:
      • If a lawyer engages in criminal acts that reflect adversely on the lawyer's honesty, trustworthiness, or fitness as a lawyer, then the lawyer commits professional misconduct. Model Rule 8.4(b).
      • If a lawyer engages in conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice or states or implies an ability to influence a government official or agency, then the lawyer commits professional misconduct. Model Rule 8.4(d); (e).
      • A lawyer must be truthful when dealing with others on a client's behalf. Model Rule 4.1.
      • A lawyer must not assert frivolous claims. Model Rule 3.1.
      • A lawyer must not counsel a client to engage, or assist a client, in conduct that the lawyer knows is criminal or Model Rule 1.2(d).

    Attorney-Client Relationship: Termination

    Terminating the Attorney-Client Relationship

    • A lawyer must end representation if (a) continued representation will violate the rules of professional conduct, (b) the lawyer's physical or mental condition materially impairs her ability to represent the client, or (c) the lawyer is discharged. Model Rule 1.16(a).
    • A lawyer may end representation if one of the following conditions are met:
      • withdrawal does not have a materially adverse effect on the client's interests;
      • the client persists in a course of action involving the lawyer's services that lawyer reasonably believes is criminal or fraudulent;
      • the client has used the lawyer's services to perpetrate crime or fraud;
      • the client insists upon taking action that the lawyer considers repugnant or with which the lawyer has a fundmanetal disagreement;
      • the client fails substantially to fulfill an obligation to the lawyer regarding the lawyer's services and has been given reasonable warning that the lawyer will withdraw unless the obligation is fulfilled;
      • the representation will unreasonably financially burden the lawyer;
      • the client has rendered the representation unreasonably difficult; or
      • other good cause of withdrawal exists.

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ is approached by client $y$ for help purchasing an expensive house with cash.
      • $y$ is a citizen of country known for corruption, and has no previous professional relationship with $x$.
      • $y$ tells $x$ he wants to structure the purchase so it is diffcult, if not impossible, for someone to find out that $y$ purchased the property.
      • $x$ asks why, to which $x$ says: "I need a place to store some money that I do not want my governet to find or trace back to me."

        • $y$ will be the sole shareholder of a corporation;
        • said corporation will own another corporation;
        • and that corporation will purchase the house with cash.
      • Unknown to $x$, the money that $y$ is using to purchase the house is stolen money.

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • Yes. An attorney has a duty of rejecting representation when the prospective client intends to use the lawyer's services to engage in moneylaundering activities. Model Rules 1.16(a); 1.2(d).

    • Hypothetical: Terminating the Attorney-Client Relationship

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represented client $y$ in a divorce proceeding.
      • $x$ and $y$ had several disagreements about the appropriate tactics, and $y$ stopped paying $x$.
      • After several weeks, $y$ decided she wanted a different attorney, so she fired $x$.
      • After giving $y$ sufficient notice to obtain new counsel, $x$ requested the court to withdraw representation of $y$.
      • The court, however, denied the request, so $x$ continued representing $y$.

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • No, because an attorney is required to remain on the case if a court denies an attorney permission to withdraw.     • Hypothetical: Terminating the Attorney-Client Relationship

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ has represented client $y$ for more than a year on a large real estate project.
      • $x$ has grown increasingly concerned about the adequacy of disclosures $y$ plans to make to government agencies.

    Discipline

    • Illustration: Duty of Competent Representation I

      Facts

      • Client $y$ retains attorney $x$ for help filing a patent.
      • $y$ informs $x$ that time was of the essence, because there were competitors.
      • $x$ spent a significant amount of time researching $y$'s potential patent, and concluded that $y$ had a reasonable chance of success.
      • $x$ then sent a bill to $y$, but, through no fault of $y$, the bills were never delivered.
      • During the 6 months that $x$ was not paid, $x$ did no work on $y$'s matter.
      • On the 7th month, $y$ called $x$ and askied if the patent had been awarded.
      • $x$ informed $y$ that he had done no work on $y$'s matter, because $y$ had not paid his bill.
      • $y$ hand-delivered a check that same day.
      • Receiving the check, $x$ resumed work on the patent.
      • The $x$ checked, and saw that no one had filed a similar or identical patent during the past 6 months.
      • $x$ then filed the patent application and other necessary forms, allowing $y$ to issue her invention with the label "patent pending."

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Time was of the essence, and $x$ did not work on the matter for 6 months. Rule 1.1 requires a lawyer to provide competent representation, which requires the legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation reasonably necessary for the representation. The fact that the client had not paid his bill is no excuse—as long as an attorney-relationship exists, an attorney has a non-waivable obligation to provide competent representation per Rule 1.1, and a duty to provide diligent representation, per Rule 1.3. Moreover, the fact that the client did not suffer any harm does not excuse these duties, since Rule 1.1 does not include a harm exception or requirement.
    • Illustration: Duty of Competent Representation II

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ is a partner at a law firm specializing in family law, and himself specializes in family law.
      • Late at night, $x$ receives a phone call from existing client $y$, who had been arrested and jailed.
      • $y$ informed $x$ that he was claustrophobic and begged $x$ to get him out.
      • $x$ informed $y$ that he had no criminal law expertise and did not handle criminal cases.
      • $y$ told $x$ that he had used his one free phone call and pleaded with $x$ for help.
      • $x$ proceeded to the police station to try and secure $y$'s release.
      • Because of his lack of experience, $x$ was unable to secure the release.
      • The next morning, $x$ found an experienced criminal lawyer who obtained $y$'s release within an hour.

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • No. Because neither referral to nor consultation with another lawyer was practical under the circumstances, $x$ did the best she could in an emergency situation. Even if $x$ did not have the legal knowledge and skill necessary to handling the criminal case, this was an emergency situation, qualifying under the emergency exception to Rule 1.1. Moreover, even if the lawyer was unable to secure $y$'s release, Rule 1.1. focuses on the services that a lawyer must provide, not the results that a client must receive.
    • Illustration: Imputed Duty of Competent Representation

      Facts

      • Attorney $w$ is a partner at a law firm that focuses on a wide variety of legal fields. The firm hired associate $v$, a fresh law school graduate who had recently passed the bar exam.
      • Since joining the firm, $v$ has worked exclusively in trusts and estates matters, supervised by a firm partner with the relevant experise.
      • The firm lost a lawyer, and $w$ informed $v$ that he was to try a case the following the week.
      • $v$ protested, arguing that he had no experience in trial litigation.
      • $w$ ignored the concerns, and $v$ went to court.
      • $v$ diligently prepared the case, but many of her questions were in improper form, and the judge repeatedly sustained evidentiary objections that opposing counsel raised.
      • At trial's conclusion, the jury returned with a guilty verdict against the firm's client.

      Question

      • Is $w$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Under Rule 5.1(b), $w$ had a duty to make reasonably efforts to ensure that $v$'s conduct conformed to the Rules of Professional Conduct–in this case, the duty to provide competent representation. Even if we do not know whether $v$'s conduct caused the jury to return the guilty verdict, Rule 1.1 can be violated even in the absence of a showing of a harm to the client. While causation and harm are necessary elements in a legal malpractice claim, they are not required for establishing a professional conduct violation.
    • Illustration: Law Firm Malpractice Liability

      Facts

      • Transactional attorney $x$ regularly advises closely-held corporations on tax and corporate issues.
      • An acquaintance $y$ asked $x$ if they could meet to discuss a legal matter, and $x$ agreed.
      • At the meeting, the $y$ asked if $x$ could represent him on a trusts and estate problem.
      • $x$ informed $y$ that he had no experience in the field, and that he was too busy with other matters to provide competent representation.
      • $x$ instead offered $y$ a referral to another, experienced lawyer, $z$.
      • $y$ did not contact $z$ until 8 months after speaking with $x$.
      • After speaking with $z$, $x$ learned that his method of handling the estate's creditors had created an enormous legal and financial problem, that could have been avoided had he received legal advice at the time he spoke with $x$.

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to civil liability?

      Analysis

      • No. $x$ did not owe any duty to $y$. Even if $x$ met with $y$, malpractice law does not impose on a lawyer a duty of care with respect to every potential client that the lawyer initially consults with. Moreover, even if it would have been helpful for $x$ to inform $y$ of the matter's urgency, an attorney is not obligated to provide an initial consult with a specified date to contact a referred lawyer. Note, however, that the fact that an initial consult declines an attorney's referral offer is irrelevant to whether an attorney commits malpractice.
    • Illustration: Limiting Malpratice Liability by Agreement I

      Facts

      • Client $y$ asks attorney $x$ to represent her in a patent infringment suit.
      • $x$ reasonably believes that he can provide competent representation, but he does not have sufficient insurance coverage in the event of malpractice.
      • Therefore, $x$ asks $y$ to agree to an upper limit of $20M on any potential malpractice claim.
      • $x$ shows $y$ the proposed agreement, and fully discusses the consequences of agreeing to the term.
      • $x$ also recommends that $y$ consult with independent counsel before signing the agreement.
      • $y$, however, decides to sign the agreement without independent counsel, explaining that he did not want to pay the legal fees of another lawyer.
      • Such an agreement is not prohibited by law in the $y$'s jurisdiction.

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Under Rule 1.8(h)(1), for an attorney to offer and enter into such an agreement, the client must be independently represented. Even if the client gave informed consent and decided not to obtain independent counsel, it is misconduct for the attorney to enter into the agreement without the client being independently reprsented. The key point is, a lawyer and a client are free to enter into an agreement limiting the lawyer's malpractice liability, but only if the client is independently represented.
    • Illustration: Limiting Malpractice Liability by Agreement II

      Facts

      • Client $y$ is in a car accident, retains attorney $x$.
      • $x$ and $y$ negotiated and signed a settlement agreement with $y$'s insurance company, where $y$ provided a general release of her personal injury protection ("PIP") benefits.
      • After the agreement, $y$ received a medical bill that the insurance company would normally pay.
      • $x$ discovered that she had been negligent in drafting the settlement agreement; a competent lawyer would not have had their client sign a general release of PIP benefits.
      • $x$ discloses this fact to $y$, and offers that if $y$ would settle a malpractice claim against him, he would reimburse $y$ for the medical bill and for any future medical bills.
      • $x$ reasonably believed that this was a fair agreement, and advised $y$ to seek independent counsel before entering into the agreement, and gave $y$ reasoanble time to retain such counsel.
      • $y$ did not obtain independent counsel, but signed the agreement.

      Question

      • Was $x$'s conduct proper?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Under Rule 1.8(h)(2), a lawyer is permitted to offer and enter into a settlement for malpractice liability with a client, as long as the client is advised in writing to seek independent counsel and is given a reasonable opportunity to seek the advice of independent counsel. Moreover, the fact that the $x$ initiated the settlement discussions or drafted the settlement agreement is irrelevant; Rule 1.8(h)(2) does not require the terms of the settlement to be fair, and it does not prohibit an attorney from drafing a malpractice settlement agreement. Finally, Rule 1.8(h)(2) prohibits agreements prospectively limiting malpractice claims; it does not require a client to be separately represented in negotiating and finalizing a settlement agreement.
    • Illustration: Agreements Limiting the Scope of Representation

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ is a specialist in e-discovery, and is retained by client $y$ to frame e-discovery requests and responses in a complex lawsuit.
      • $x$ offers $y$ an agreement specifying that $x$'s representation is limited to advice about e-discovery requests.
      • $x$ fully advises $y$, and $y$ signs the agreement.

      Question

      • Is $x$'s conduct proper?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Under Rule 1.2(c), a lawyer is permitted to "limit the scope of the representation if the limitation is reasonable under the circumstances and the client gives informed consent." It is irrelevant whether the lawyer or the client initiates the request for limited representation. Furthermore, Rule 1.8(h)(1), restricting the ability to limit prospective liability, must be read in the context of Rule 1.2(c). Finally, even if an attorney normally works with a client directly, rather than with the client's other attorneys, nothing in Rule 1.2(c) prohibits a lawyer from agreeing to work on a specific issue.
    • Illustration: Allocating Decision-Making Between Client & Lawyer I

      Facts

      • Client $y$ retains attorney $x$ for recovery from a personal injury.
      • In the retainer agreement (signed by the $y$ and $x$), $y$ agreed to cooperate fully and to pay $x$ a contingent fee.
      • The contingent fee was to be a percentage of the amount of recovery $y$ would received after expenses:
        • If settled before trial, 25%.
        • If setteled before verdict, 30%.
        • If settled after verdict, 35%.
        • If settled after appeal, 40%.
      • $x$ represented $y$ for three years, during which $x$ advanced a large amount for litigation expenses.
      • After trial, the jury returned an award larger than either $x$ or $y$ anticipated.
      • The defendant, however, appealed the verdict based on evidentiary and damage measurement questions.
      • Meanwhile, the defendant made an offer of settlement for about the same amount that $x$ anticipated.
      • $y$, financially unstable, directed $x$ to accept the offer and settle.
      • Confident that there was no reversible error in trial and that the appeal was without merit, refused the settlement, believing that the appeal was purely a negotiation tactic.

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Under Rule 1.2, the only client decision in a civil matter expressly identified as an objective is the decision of whether to settle. Therefore, $x$ was obligated to comply with the $y$'s decision to accept the settlement offer. While a lawyer is free to enter a contingency fee agreement that lists different percentages depending on when the case is resolved, if a client says she wants to settle, the lawyer must comply. Furthermore, even though an evaluation of an appeal's mertis requires an attorney to exercise independent professional judgment, that requirement does not give the attorney a right to decide whether or not to settle.
    • Illustration: Allocating Decision-Making Between Client & Lawyer II

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents client $y$ in a divorce proceeding; $y$ wants primary custody of her children.
      • $y$ instructs $x$ not to use evidence of her spouse's adultery.
      • $x$ informs $y$ that the adultery evidence would be extremely helpful in gaining primary custody and avoiding joint custody.
      • $y$, however, insists that $x$ not introduce such evidence.
      • $x$ followed $y$'s instructions, and did not introduce the evidence.
      • The court denied $y$ primary custody, and awarded instead joint custody.
      • $y$ then sued $x$ for malpractice, based on $x$'s failure to introduce evidence of adultery.

      Question

      • Is $y$'s claim likely to succeed?

      Analysis

      • No. A lawyer who has explained the alternatives to a client, to which the client instructs the lawyer not to pursue such alternatives, is not liable for malpractice in not pursuing such alternatives—the lawyer simply followed the client's instructions. Legal malpractice liability is not warranted if the lawyer merely followed the client's directions. Note, however, that a lawyer is not required to follow all of a client's instructions—the lawyer must comply with the Rules of Professional Conduct. Further note that the issue of malpractice liability is different from professional conduct; the fact that whether to introduce evidence is a "means" rather than an "objective" is irrelevant to determining the success of a malpractice claim.
    • Illustration: Advising a Client on Potentially Criminal Conduct

      Facts

      • Federal law requires self-employed individuals to make estimated quarterly federal tax payments.
      • If an individual fails to do so, she is subject to a misdemeanor with significant civil penalties. While a criminal misdemeanor charge is possible, the IRS normally pursues civil penalties.
      • Attorney $x$ represents a small business owner $y$ who finds the quarterly payments a nuisance.
      • When $y$ pays his annual tax return, he willingly pays the model civil penalty for failing to make the quarterly payments.
      • $y$ calls $x$, and asks whether any taxpayers had been criminally prosecuted for failing to make the payments.
      • $y$ also asked $x$ if there was anything a taxpayer could do to minimize the risk of detection.
      • $x$ accurately responded that he did not know of any criminal prosecutions for the offense, and he also informed $y$ that it was improper for him to advise on how to avoid detection.
      • $y$ thanked $x$, and hung up.
      • Later that year, while preparing $y$'s tax returns, $x$ noticed that $y$ had not made any estimated quarterly tax payments.

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • No. While $x$ arguably should have known what was going on, Rule 1.2(d) prohibits a lawyer from counseling or assisting a client to engage in conduct that the lawyer knows is criminal or fraudulent. The rule does not use a reasonably should have known mens rea, nor does it focus on the outcome of advice that "encourages" a client to violate the law. Furthermore, Rule 1.2(d) does not require a lawyer to discourage her client from breaking the law. $x$ was proper in declining to discuss methods of avoiding detection, and because $x$'s advice concerned the consequences that would likely result if the client violated the law (which is permitted by Rule 1.2(d)), $x$ is not subject to discipline.

      Facts

      • $y$ informed $x$ that he could set aside time in October, November, or December for the trial.

      Question

      • Was it proper for $x$ to set the trial date without obtaining $y$'s consent?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Under Rule 1.2(a), a lawyer may pursue means on behalf of the client as is impliedly authorized to carry out the representation. Under Rest. § 23, cmt. d., lawyers have the inherent authority to act and decide for their clients when the legal system requires an immediate decision without time for consultation.       • Lawyer $x$ is retained by client $y$ for a family law matter.

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ agreed to represent plaintiff $y$ in a personal injury matter.
      • Orignally, both $x$ and $y$ agreed to a 30% contingent fee, which was a reasonable fee for the type of cases that $x$ handled.
      • 1 year into litigation, $x$ grew increasingly busy, and there was a long waitlist of potential clients.

      • $x$ and $y$'s agreement was silent on the possibility of a fee increase.

      • $x$ explained that if $y$ did not agree, $x$ would withdraw representation and find $y$ another experienced personal injury attorney charging at the original fee.
      • $y$ reluctantly agreed to modify the fee agreement, as proposed.
      • Thereafter, $y$'s case was settled.

      • As a result, $x$ sued $y$ to recover the agreed modified contingent fee.

      • Is $x$ likely to prevail?

      Analysis

      • To recover the modified contingent fee, an attorney must show that "the client was not pressured to accede in order to avoid the problems of changing counsel, alienating the lawyer, missing a deadline or losing a significant opportunity in the matter, or because a new lawyer would have to repeat significant work for which the client owned or had paid the first lawyer." In other words, "the lawyer must show that a reasonable client might have chosen to accept the late contract, typically because it benefited the client in some substantial way (other than by relieving the client from having to find a new lawyer)." Rest. § 18, cmt. e. Modifications sought by a lawyer that change the basic nature of a fee arrangement or significantly increase the lawyer's compensation absent an unanticipated change in circumstances ordinarily will be unreasonable. See ABA Formal Opinion 11-458. Here, it is unlikely for $x$ to recover the contingent fee, because the possibility of increases was not communicated to the client at the beginning of the attorney-client relationship. Furthermore, it is likely that the lawyer knew, or should have known, that his client waitlist was going to grow longer.

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents client $y$ in a complext product liability litigation.
      • Trial preparation expenses are gargantuan, and $y$ is highly financially unstable.
      • $x$ is aware of a company that might be willing to make a non-recourse loan to $y$, provided that $y$ guarantees the loan with future settlement proceeds.
      • $x$ considers assisting $y$ securing this loan.

      Question

      • Is $x$ permitted to assist $y$ in securing the loan?

      Analysis

      • It depends on the jurisdiction. In Kentucky, and many other jurisdictions, lawyers are not per se prohibited from assisting clients in obtaining financing from a third-party lender. See Kentucky Bar Ethics Opinion E-342. Lawyers may provide such assistance, but they must be cognizant of potential misunderstandings as to the lawyer's role; this can be avoided by providing written confirmation. Id. Some jurisdictions, like New York, prohibit a lawyer providing such assistance if the lawyer is an investor in the client company. See New York State Ethics Opinion 1145 (2018). Thus, here, $x$ may be permitted to provide such assistanceto $y$, depending on the jurisdiction.
    • Illustration: Agreement Dividing Fee

      Facts

      • In a medical malpractice case, attorney $x$ represents client $y$.
      • In $x$ and $y$'s fee agreement, $y$ pays for $x$'s legal services in the form of a contingent fee:
        • 20% of the recovery if the case is settled;
        • 30% of the recover if the case is tried; and
        • the total fee will not exceed $50,000.
      • To aid in litigation, $x$ enlists attorney $w$, a solo practitioner; $y$ provided written consent after full disclosure of the fee agreement between $x$ and $w$.
      • $w$ is both a doctor and a lawyer, and is well-qualified to try medical malpractice cases.
      • $x$ and $w$'s fee agreement provides:
        • Total fee is:
          • 20% of the recovery if the case is settled;
          • 30% of the recover if the case is tried; and
          • the total fee will not exceed $50,000.
        • $x$ will help with discovery, and will be the liaison between $y$ and $w$.
        • If the case is not settled before trial, $w$ will prepare and try the case, to which $x$ and $y$ will divide the fee, 40% to $x$ and 60% to $w$.

      Question

      • Are $x$ and $w$ subject to discipline this fee dividing agreement?

      Analysis

      • Under Rule 1.5(e), lawyers not in the same firm may share fees only if: (a) the division is proportionate to the services each lawyer performs or each lawyer assumes joint responsibility for the representation; (b) the client agrees to the arrangement, including the share each lawyer will receive, and the agreement is confirmed in writing; and (c) the total fee is reasonable. Here, it is likely that these requirements are met, so $x$ and $w$ are not subject to discipline for this fee dividing agreement.
    • Illustration: Accepting Payment from a Client's Relative

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ is retained by client $y$ for a custody proceeding.
      • $y$'s aunt, $z$, hoping for a positive outcome, sent $x$ a $5,000 check, instructing $y$ to take it as payment for his services to $y$.
      • $z$ further asks $x$ not to inform $y$ of the payment, because $y$ is too proud to accept gifts from relatives, even when he needs the money.

      Question

      • Can $x$ properly accept $z$'s check?

      Analysis

      • Under Rule 1.8(f), a lawyer cannot accept compensation for representing a client from anyone other than the client unless: (1) the client gives informed consent; (2) there is no interference with the lawyer's independence of professional judgment or with the client-lawyer relationship; and (3) the lawyer protects the client's confidential information. Furthermore, under Rule 5.4(c), a lawyer must not permit a person who recommends, employs, or pays the lawyer to render legal services for another to direct or regulate her professional judgment in rendering legal services. Here, $y$ has not given informed consent. Thus, $x$ cannot properly accept $z$'s check.
    • Illustration: Trust Accounting I

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ received a $10,000 retainer from client $y$ for a new matter.
      • $x$ promptly placed $y$'s payment in her client trust account.
      • $x$ expected doing at least 10 hours of work per week on $y$'s matter over the next 4 weeks.
      • $x$'s standard rate is $250/hour.
      • To cover upcoming office expenses, $x$ transferred \$2,000 of anticipated earnings from $y$'s trust account into a business account.
      • $x$ then explained to $y$ that the \$2,000 had been moved, to which $y$ approved.
      • As expected, $x$ did in fact work 10 hours on $y$'s matter for four weeks.

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Under Rule 1.15, advanced fees paid by a client must remain in a trust account until the lawyer actually earns the fees or expenses are incurred. $x$ absolutely cannot transfer funds in $y$'s trust account until she earns those fees. While lawyers are permitted to use client funds for business expenses, they are permitted to do so only if the expenses have incurred. Here, the expenses have not incurred. Furthermore, the fact that $y$ approved of the transfer does not exclude $x$ from Rule 1.15's prohibition. Finally, the fact that $x$ accurately predicted the amount of hours he was going to work is not a defense against Rule 1.15.
    • Illustration: Trust Accounting II

      Facts

      • Client $y$ paid attorney $x$ a \$10,000 retainer for defense in a property dispute.
      • $x$ promptly deposited the funds into a client trust account.
      • 1 day after receiving the retainer, $x$ spent about 10 hours on $y$'s matter, primarily conduct research.
      • Shortly after research, $y$ sends $x$ a message: "My neighbor just agreed to settle, please stop working on my matter."
      • $x$ then sent $y$ a bill for \$2,000 for his 10 hours of work on the matter, based on his standard hourly rate of $200/hour.
      • $y$ refused to pay the bill, and demanded a full refund of the \$10,000, alleging that $x$'s work was unnecessary.

      Question

      • What should $x$ do with the \$10,000?

      Analysis

      • Per Rule 1.15(e), while representing a client, if the lawyer possesses property where two or more persons (including the lawyer) claim interests, the lawyer must keep the property separate until the dispute is resolved. For all other property where interests are not in dispute, such property must be promptly returned to their respective owners. Here, \$2,000 is in dispute, but \$8,000 is not. Thus, the \$2,000 must be left in the client trust account until the dispute is resolved, but the \$8,000 must be returned to the client promptly. Note what this means: The lawyer must not return the entire \$10,000 to the client in an attempt to "make the problem go away." The amount must be kept in the trust account until the dispute is resolved.
    • Illustration: Refunding Unearned Fees

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents client $y$ in an immigration matter for a nonrefundable flat fee of \$5,000.
      • $y$ pays the fee in advance, and $x$ deposits the advanced fee into a business account.
      • Several weeks later, while the representation is still in its preliminary stages, $y$ grows frustrated with with $x$'s work, and hires new counsel, $z$.
      • $z$ requests $x$ to provide $y$'s files and a refund of any unearned fees.
      • $x$ refuses, stating that the fee was nonrefundable.

      Question

      • Is $x$ subject to discipline?

      Analysis

      • Yes. Several rule violations have occurred here. First, $y$'s payment was an advance fee; $x$ had not yet earned it. As such, $x$ should have deposited the money into a trust account, per Rule 1.15(c), rather than a business account. Second, a substantial part of the fees likely have not yet been earned; as such, those fees must be returned to $y$, per Rule 1.16(d). Given that $y$ has likely discharged $x$, $x$ must return $y$'s file.
    • Illustration: Court-awarded Attorney's Fees

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents a class of plaintiffs in a civil rights case against the State of Utopia.
      • Utopia offers to provide substantially all of the reliefs plaintiffs sought, as long as the attorney agreed to waive court-awarded attorney's fees under 42 U.S.C. § 1988.
      • $x$ agreed to waiver her fee award, and the parties settled.
      • The plaintiffs later appealed the settlement, arguing that the demand for a waiver of court-awarded attorney's fees undermined the important public policy goal of encouraging lawyers to undertake such representations.

      Question

      • What likely appeals court outcome?

      Analysis

      • The court will likely uphold the settlement because 28 USC § 1988 does not prohibit settlement conditioned on a waiver of attorney's fees. See Evans v. Jeff D., 475 U.S. 717 (1986).
    • Illustration: Gossiping About a Client

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents client $y$ in a personal injury suit after being recently hit by a car.
      • During his investigation, $x$ comes across a surveillance tape, one minute before the accident, where $y$ had exited from a porn shop with an unknown woman.
      • At a dinner party, $x$ tells everyone the how $y$ got run over immediately after exiting a porn shop.

      Question

      • Was $x$'s conduct proper?

      Analysis

      • No. $x$ clearly violated the duty of confidentiality by disclosing information related to a client's representation. $x$'s disclosure is not protected by any of the exceptions. The fact that someone was with $y$ at the time does not negate the duty of confidentiality. Unlike attorney-client privilege, the duty of confidentiality is not contingent upon the information being a secret communication between the lawyer and the client.
    • Illustration: Demand for Evidence from Opposing Counsel

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents client $y$, who suffered injuries at a construction site.
      • During her investigation, $x$ finds a surveillance tape showing $y$, one minute before the accident, horsing around with another worker, $z$, near dangerous equipment.
      • Before trial, $x$ receives a discovery request from defense counsel, demanding producing of any surveillance tapes that are or may be relevant to the action.
      • $x$ refuses to produce the surveillance tape.

      Question

      • Is $x$ conduct proper?

      Analysis

      • No. Under Rule 1.6(b)(6), a lawyer must comply with other law or a court order. Here, the information on the surveillance tape is not privileged, since the communication was not secret ($y$ was horsing around with $z$), and it was not a communication made between $y$ and $x$. Because information on the surveillance tape is not privileged, $x$ must turn over the non-privileged information. What about the duty of confidentiality? An attorney does not violate the duty of confidentiality when reveal information through discovery after a lawful demand for such information has been made.
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege I

      Facts

      • Client $y$ retains attorney $x$ for assistance in a real estate transaction.
      • $y$ asks $x$ in confidence whether $x$ thinks it is "a good and workable deal."

      Question

      • Is this communication privileged?

      Analysis

      • Yes. A communication between a client and counsel is privileged if the communication's dominant intent is to seek legal advice. The fact that the client asked if the deal was "good and workable" indicates this to be case. Note: For the purposes of attorney-client privilege, the fact that a client spoke in confidence to counsel is insufficient to establish a privileged communication; the dominant intent must be to seek legal advice.
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege II

      Facts

      • Employee $z$ of large corporation $y$ frequently works on possible new acquisitions.
      • Much of the information that employee works with is sensitive and could be harmful to the corporation if it falls into the hands of a competitor outside the corporation.
      • $z$'s co-worker tells $z$ that if $z$ copies in-house counsel when emailing sensitive documents, there will be an extra layer of protection because the email will then be privileged.

      Question

      • Is $z$'s co-worker correct?

      Analysis

      • No. Attorney-client privilege only applies to communications made with the dominant intent of securing legal advice. $z$'s emailing such documents does not meet this requirement.
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege III

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents client $y$ in a personal injury action.
      • $y$ is in a bodycast, claiming his injuries were the result of a car accident.
      • 1 month before trial, $x$ goes on a skiing vacation.
      • There, he sees $x$, without a bodycast, skiing happily down a slope.
      • The adverse party, $z$, now subpoenas $x$ to provide any information $x$ has about the $y$'s medical condition.
      • $x$, who has since withdrawn from representation, refuses to supply any information and invokes both attorney-client privilege and the duty of confidentiality.

      Question

      • Is $x$'s conduct proper?

      Facts

      • No. $x$ cannot refuse to tesify to the information he gathered while observing $y$ on the ski slopes. This observation was not a communication made in confidence between the lawyer and the client, or agents of either who facilitate communications between them. Furthermore, the duty of confidentiality does not apply here. Under Rule 1.6(b)(6), an attorney is permitted to disclose confidences "to comply with other law or a court order."
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege IV

      Facts

      • The board of directors of client corporation $y$ votes to do a public offering of stock.
      • In furtherance of the offering, $y$ hires law firm $w$ to prepare the necessary documentation.
      • Attorney $x$, a lawyer outside the firm, communicates with the $y$'s corporate personnel and obtains factual information that would put $y$ in a negative light.
      • After reviewing this information, $x$ recommends that $y$ terminate its plan to proceed with a public offering.
      • $y$'s board of directors agrees.

      Question

      • If the information was subpoenaed, would the negative information obtained by the attorney be protected by attorney-client privilege?

      Analysis

      • No. $y$ provided the information to $x$ for the purpose of making it public, not with an expectation of privacy.
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege V

      Facts

      • Client corporation $y$ is being investigated by the DOJ for pollution.
      • Before the DOJ investigation began, $y$ hired attorney $x$ to investigate how $y$ disposing of the chemicals.
      • $x$ interviewed $y$'s corporate agents and filed a report with $y$.
      • The DOJ requests this report from $y$.
      • $y$ agrees, but only if the DOJ signs a confidentiality agreement where the DOJ will not turn over the report to any private parties.
      • After receiving the report, the DOJ concludes its investigation.
      • Private parties alleging to have been injured by $y$'s conduct are now suing $y$.
      • The parties serve a discovery demand for the report; $y$ refuses, citing attorney-client privilege.

      Question

      • Is the report privileged?

      Analysis

      • No. The corporation waived attorney-client privilege when it disclosed the report to the DOJ. The fact that $y$ entered into a contract with the DOJ to keep the report confidential is irrelevant; there is no distinction between general and selective waivers.
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege VI

      Facts

      • Corporation $z$ sued corporation client $y$, alleging fraud.
      • Both $z$ and $y$ sought discovery of thousands of emails relevant to the case.
      • At $y$'s request, and over $z$'s objection, the court entered an order providing that any disclosure of information protected by the attorney-client privilege during discovery would not constitute a waiver of the privilege.
      • After the order was entered, $y$ produced 800,000 emails; 10,000 of those emails involved confidential attorney-client communications.
      • Subsequently, $y$ was sued for fraud by a third party corporation, $g$, which was involved in the contract originating the fraud.
      • $g$ sought to use as evidence the 1,000 privileged emails that $y$ produced in response to $z$, arguing that $y$ waived the privilege by producing them in that litigation.

      Question

      • Are $y$'s emails privileged in the second case?

      Analysis

      • Yes. A court order protects against a waiver in any subsequent litigation. Under FRE 502(d), a federal court may order that the privilege or protection is not waived by disclosure connected with the litigation pending before the court—in which event the disclosure is also not a waiver in any other federal or state proceeding.
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege VII

      Facts

      • Attorney $x$ represents client $y$, a used care salesman.
      • $y$ has been sued by buyer $z$, $z$ claiming that she bought a car from $y$ with a turned-back odometer.
      • When $x$ asks $y$ to describe the background of the dispute, the client makes the following statements in confidence:
        • "I've destroyed documents that could be used to prove $z$'s case."
        • "I still think that there might be some documents in the files that could be relevant to the buyer's case. Could you look through the files and let me know what you think the buyer might ask for in discovery?"

      Question

      • Are $y$'s statements privileged?

      Analysis

      • No. Under the crime-fraud exception, privilege does not apply to a communication if the communication was made with the intent to further an ongoing or future crime or fraud, even if the lawyer is unaware of the client's intent. Rest. § 82(b).
    • Illustration: Attorney-Client Privilege VIII

      Facts

      • Medium sized law firm $x$ represents a difficult client, $y$.
      • Since the representation began, $y$ has complained about $z$'s, a lawyer at $y$, services, and is now stating that $y$ will not pay any more fees, but expects $x$ to finish the case and will probably sue $x$ when the case concludes.
      • $z$ frequently speaks with $w$, $x$'s informal ethics counsel, about how to deal with $y$.
      • $x$ decides to withdraw representation, to which $y$ sues and demands production of all communications between $z$ and $w$.

      Question

      • Are the communications between $z$ and $w$ privileged?

      Analysis

      • It depends on the jurisdiction. In jurisdictions like New York, there is such a thing as intra-firm privilege, which functions much like attorney-client privilege. See Stock v. Schnader Harrison Segal & Lewis LLP, 35 N.Y.S.3d 31 (App. Div. 2016).
